## 1. Legislative Background

### The Fair Housing Act & Familial Status Protection

The **Fair Housing Act of 1968** (as amended in 1988) prohibits discrimination in housing based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability, and **familial status**. Familial status protections cover households with one or more persons under 18 living with a parent or legal guardian, pregnant persons, and those in the process of obtaining custody of a minor.

### The Housing for Older Persons Act (HOPA)

**HOPA** (Public Law 104-76), signed into law on **December 28, 1995**, amended the Fair Housing Act to create a streamlined exemption for senior housing communities. It replaced the previous "significant facilities and services" requirement from the 1988 Fair Housing Amendments Act with a simpler three-part test.

Key change from prior law: HOPA **eliminated** the requirement that 55+ communities provide "significant facilities and services designed to meet the physical or social needs of older persons." This had been a major compliance burden and source of litigation.

### Implementing Regulations

HUD published the final rule implementing HOPA at **24 CFR Part 100, Subpart E** (sections 100.300 through 100.308), effective **May 3, 1999**. These remain the operative federal regulations.

**Statutory authority:** 42 U.S.C. § 3607(b)(2)(C)

## 2. Three Categories of Exempt Housing

Under the Fair Housing Act as amended by HOPA, three types of housing are exempt from familial status discrimination claims:

### A. State/Federal Elderly Housing Programs (§ 100.302)
Housing provided under any federal or state program specifically designed and operated to assist elderly persons (e.g., HUD Section 202 Supportive Housing for the Elderly).

### B. 62+ Housing (§ 100.303)
Housing intended for and **solely occupied** by persons 62 years of age or older.
- **Every** resident must be 62 or older
- Exception: Employees performing substantial management/maintenance duties may be under 62
- Grandfathered occupants: Persons under 62 residing as of September 13, 1988 may remain, but all new occupants must be 62+

### C. 55+ Housing (§ 100.304) — The HOPA Exemption
Housing intended and operated for persons 55 years of age or older, meeting three specific requirements (detailed below). This is the most commonly used exemption for community associations.

## 3. 55+ Community Requirements (Three Prongs)

To qualify for the 55+ exemption under HOPA, a community must satisfy **all three** requirements simultaneously:

### Prong 1: The 80% Occupancy Rule
At least 80% of occupied units must be occupied by at least one person who is 55 years of age or older.

### Prong 2: Intent to Operate as 55+ Housing
The community must publish and adhere to policies and procedures that demonstrate its intent to operate as housing for persons 55 years of age or older.

### Prong 3: Age Verification Compliance
The community must comply with HUD's regulatory requirements for verification of occupancy through reliable surveys and affidavits.

**Failure to meet even one of these requirements causes the community to lose its legal right to exclude families with children.**

## 4. The 80% Occupancy Rule — Detailed

**Regulatory basis:** 24 CFR § 100.305

### Core Rule
At least **80% of occupied units** must have at least one occupant who is 55 years of age or older. The remaining 20% may be occupied by persons under 55.

### Key Definitions

**"Occupied unit"** means:
- A dwelling unit actually occupied by one or more persons on the date the exemption is claimed; OR
- A temporarily vacant unit, if the primary occupant has resided there during the past year and intends to return on a periodic basis

**"Occupied by at least one person 55+"** means:
- At least one occupant is 55+ on the date the exemption is claimed; OR
- If temporarily vacant, at least one occupant immediately prior to vacancy was 55+

### Calculating the 80%

Start with total occupied units, then **exclude** from the calculation:
1. Units continuously occupied by the same household since September 13, 1988, that did not and do not contain anyone 55+
2. Unoccupied units (these are excluded entirely from both numerator and denominator)
3. Units occupied by employees under 55 who perform substantial management/maintenance duties
4. Units occupied solely by persons providing medical/health/nursing care as a reasonable accommodation to disabled residents

Then calculate: Of the remaining units, what percentage are occupied by at least one person 55+? This must be at least 80%.

### Special Situations

- **Fractional units:** When 80% results in a fraction, that unit is included in the units that must be occupied by someone 55+
- **New construction:** Need not comply with 80% rule until at least 25% of units are occupied
- **Temporary absence:** A 55+ occupant on vacation, hospitalized, or absent for a season does not affect the count. A house-sitter under 55 may stay during this absence.
- **The 80% is a minimum:** Communities may require 100% of units to have a 55+ occupant, or set any threshold at or above 80%
- **No eviction to comply:** Communities may not evict families with children to achieve the 80% threshold

### What the 20% Means

The remaining 20% of units may be occupied by persons under 55, and the community still qualifies. Communities may:
- Market the 20% to persons under 55 (but must still identify the community as intended for older persons)
- Set their own age restrictions for the non-80% units
- Impose different terms/conditions on families with children in the 20%

**Caution:** Communities should plan carefully before filling the entire 20% with under-55 households, as deaths of 55+ occupants with surviving under-55 spouses could push the community below 80%.

## 5. Intent to Operate as 55+ Housing

**Regulatory basis:** 24 CFR § 100.306

### Required: Published Policies and Procedures

The community must publish and adhere to policies and procedures demonstrating 55+ intent. HUD considers these factors:

1. **How the community is described** to prospective residents
2. **Advertising** designed to attract prospective residents
3. **Lease provisions** referencing 55+ status
4. **Written rules, regulations, covenants, deed or other restrictions** establishing 55+ operation
5. **Maintenance and consistent application** of relevant procedures
6. **Actual practices** of the community
7. **Public posting** in common areas describing the community as 55+ housing

### Critical: "Adult" Language Is Insufficient

Per 24 CFR § 100.306(b): Phrases such as **"adult living," "adult community," or similar statements** in advertisements or prospectuses **are NOT consistent** with demonstrating intent to operate as 55+ housing.

However, occasional use of "adult" language does not destroy the intent requirement if the community clearly demonstrates 55+ intent through other means, meets the 80% requirement, and has age verification procedures in place. HUD looks at the **totality of the circumstances**.

### Inconsistent Language

If community documents contain language inconsistent with 55+ intent, HUD will consider documented evidence of a good faith attempt to remove such language when evaluating compliance.

### Age Restriction in Declaration/CC&Rs

Per legal guidance (Kaman & Cusimano): The age restriction should be specified in the association's **Declaration** (recorded with the county). For most associations, this requires an amendment approved by the ownership.

## 6. Age Verification Requirements & Workflow

**Regulatory basis:** 24 CFR § 100.307

### Initial Setup

Within 180 days of the rule's effective date (or upon establishing 55+ status), the community must develop procedures for:
- Routinely determining the occupancy of each unit
- Identifying whether at least one occupant of each unit is 55+
- These procedures may be integrated into normal leasing or purchasing processes

### Acceptable Verification Documents

Any **one** of the following is sufficient (the community cannot require a specific document type):

1. **Driver's license**
2. **Birth certificate**
3. **Passport**
4. **Immigration card**
5. **Military identification**
6. **Any other official government document** (state, local, national, or international) containing a birth date of comparable reliability
7. **A self-certification** — a signed statement in a lease, application, affidavit, or other document by any household member 18+ asserting that at least one person in the unit is 55+

### Recommended Workflow (per CHDB Law)

1. **At purchase/move-in:** Require new residents to complete an age verification form and provide a copy of one acceptable document
2. **Every two years:** Conduct a biennial survey to verify continued compliance
3. **On refusal:** Use alternative evidence if residents refuse to respond
4. **Ongoing:** Maintain summaries of each survey in association records

### When Occupants Refuse to Cooperate

If occupants refuse to provide age verification, the community may consider the unit as occupied by someone 55+ **if it has sufficient evidence**, including:
- Government records or documents (e.g., local household census)
- Prior forms or applications
- A statement from a person with personal knowledge of the occupants' ages, signed under penalty of perjury

### Important Restriction (CHDB Law)

No specific document type may be required exclusively. The association is **precluded from requiring that all occupants produce a specific document** (e.g., requiring only birth certificates or only driver's licenses) to the exclusion of other acceptable forms.

## 7. Biennial Survey Requirements

**Regulatory basis:** 24 CFR § 100.307(c)

### Frequency
Age verification updates must occur **at least once every two years** (biennial).

### Content
The survey should verify:
- Current occupancy status of each unit
- Whether at least one occupant is 55+
- Whether any units are occupied by employees (under 55) performing management/maintenance
- Whether any units house persons providing reasonable accommodation services

### When Updating
- A community need not re-obtain copies of all supporting documents at each survey
- Documents that remain accurate long-term (e.g., birth certificates) need not be re-collected
- The community need only review its files and confirm that persons counted as 55+ occupants are still in occupancy

### Consequences of Failure
- Failure to conduct the biennial survey **does not demonstrate intent** to provide 55+ housing
- Could **jeopardize the community's exempt status**
- Records must be kept as long as the community intends to assert its exempt status

### Availability
A **summary of occupancy surveys** must be available for inspection upon reasonable notice and request by any person.

### Admissibility
Surveys and verification procedures complying with these requirements are **admissible in administrative and judicial proceedings** for the purpose of verifying occupancy.

## 8. Documentation Requirements

### Records the Community Must Maintain

1. **Age verification documents** for each unit (at least one acceptable form per unit)
2. **Biennial survey results** showing 80% compliance
3. **Published policies and procedures** demonstrating 55+ intent
4. **Written rules, regulations, covenants, or deed restrictions** establishing 55+ status
5. **Advertising and marketing materials** consistent with 55+ intent
6. **Public postings** in common areas
7. **Survey summaries** available for public inspection

### Retention Period
Records must be maintained **as long as the community intends to assert its exempt status** as 55+ housing.

### Burden of Proof
The **community/facility** (not the complainant) bears the burden of proving compliance with HOPA requirements on the date of any alleged discriminatory act.

## 9. Age-Restricted vs. Age-Targeted Communities

### Age-Restricted Communities (HOPA-Compliant)

- Legally qualify under HOPA as housing for older persons
- **Can legally exclude** families with children under 18
- Must meet all three HOPA requirements (80% rule, intent, verification)
- Can enforce age restrictions through rules, lease provisions, and legal action including eviction
- Still prohibited from discriminating based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, or disability
- The exemption applies **only to familial status discrimination**

### Age-Targeted Communities (NOT HOPA-Compliant)

- **Marketed toward** older adults but do not meet HOPA requirements
- **Cannot legally exclude** families with children
- May use terms like "active adult" or "55+ preferred" in marketing
- Subject to all Fair Housing Act protections including familial status
- Cannot impose different rules or restrictions on families with children
- Cannot refuse to sell or rent based on familial status
- Risk fair housing complaints if they attempt to enforce age restrictions

### Critical Legal Distinction

The difference is **legal enforceability**. An age-restricted community can deny housing and evict based on age. An age-targeted community **cannot** — any attempt to do so violates the Fair Housing Act and exposes the community to complaints, monetary damages, and legal fees.

Using "adult community" or "adult living" language alone, without meeting HOPA requirements, provides **zero legal protection** from familial status discrimination claims.

## 10. Good Faith Defense

**Regulatory basis:** 24 CFR § 100.308

### Who It Protects
Individual **natural persons** (not corporate entities, management companies, or governing boards) who acted with a good faith belief that the community qualified for the exemption.

### Requirements
1. The person must have **actual knowledge** that an authorized representative asserted in writing that the community qualifies for the exemption
2. The authorized representative must have **certified in writing, under oath or affirmation**, that the community complies before the alleged discrimination occurred

### Who Cannot Claim It
- Corporate entities, governing boards, or management companies
- Any person with **actual knowledge** that the community does not or will not qualify, regardless of written assurances received

### Who Can Rely on It
- Real estate agents, advertisers, publishers, and multiple listing services who receive and rely on the written assertions — unless they have actual knowledge the information is inaccurate
- Newspaper publishers are not liable for accepting ads for 55+ housing that later proves non-compliant, unless they had actual knowledge

## 11. CAI Best Practices & Policy Positions

### CAI's Official Policy on Fair Housing (Adopted/Amended 1988–2011)

**Source:** Community Associations Institute (CAI), Fair Housing Public Policy

Key positions:
- CAI supports the right of all individuals to be free from illegal discrimination
- CAI supports the right of community associations to enforce covenants, bylaws, and rules provided they do not illegally discriminate
- CAI encourages fair and reasonable interpretations of federal, state, and local fair housing acts

### CAI Recommendations for 55+ Communities

1. **Biennial age verification:** If managing a qualified age-restricted community, the association **must** have procedures for a biennial age verification process per HUD regulations

2. **Familiarity with multi-level law:** Associations must know local and state requirements in addition to federal law, as they may impose additional standards

3. **Board and management education:** The governing board and management must be educated on potential ramifications of fair housing violations, including **punitive damages and legal fees** (generally not covered by insurance)

4. **Sensitivity to discrimination risks:** Matters as simple as selective rules enforcement, rules that disproportionately impact children without well-documented safety basis, or suggesting a family would be "more comfortable" elsewhere may all result in successful discrimination claims

5. **Equipment and facilities:** Before posting age-related signs on equipment, confirm with the manufacturer that age-related concerns are valid

6. **Reasonable accommodations:** When a disabled person requests accommodation, seek professional guidance before dismissing the request — case law is complex and failure to honor legitimate requests has serious consequences

7. **Financial impact analysis:** When rendering decisions on accommodations, modifications, or discrimination matters, factor in the impact of adverse proceedings and judgments on association resources

### CAI on HOPA Specifically

CAI notes that HOPA:
- Modified the 1988 Amendments Act requirements
- Continues to require 80% occupancy by at least one person 55+
- Requires biennial age verification
- May provide a "good faith reliance defense or exemption against monetary damages" for familial discrimination claims

## 12. Key Scenarios & Edge Cases

### Death of 55+ Occupant with Under-55 Survivor

- Right to possession by surviving spouse or heir is governed by **state/local law and private contracts**, not HOPA or the Fair Housing Act
- The surviving under-55 occupant is counted in the **20% portion** (not the 80%)
- The 20% buffer exists partly to prevent communities from losing exempt status due to this situation

### Household Changes (Pregnancy, Custody)

If a HOPA-compliant community has a resident who becomes pregnant or gains custody of a child:
- The community **can** enforce rules prohibiting children as full-time residents
- Discretion exists: some communities allow grace periods (6 months to 1 year), temporary stays, or exceptions for guardianship
- Other communities have strict no-exceptions policies
- Residents may face notices of violation, be asked to find alternate housing, or face eviction proceedings

### Temporary Absences

A 55+ occupant may be temporarily absent (vacation, hospitalization, seasonal) without affecting the community's status, provided:
- The unit is not rented out
- The occupant returns on a periodic basis
- The occupant maintains legal and financial responsibility for the unit
- A house-sitter under 55 may stay during this period

### Live-In Caregivers/Health Aides

Units occupied solely by persons providing medical, health, or nursing care as a reasonable accommodation are **excluded from the 80% calculation entirely** — regardless of whether the caregiver lives in the same unit or a separate unit.

### Transitioning to 55+ Status

A community that doesn't currently meet the 80% threshold can:
- Reserve all new/vacant units for 55+ occupancy until reaching 80%
- Market vacant units as 55+ during the transition
- Refuse to rent/sell to families with children during the transition period (if demonstrating intent and adopting verification procedures)
- **Cannot** evict families with children to achieve compliance
- Must achieve 80% within the transition period or rescind all restrictive policies

### HUD Does Not Certify Communities

Neither the Fair Housing Act nor HOPA authorizes HUD to certify whether a particular community qualifies as housing for older persons. Compliance is self-assessed and tested only when a complaint is filed or HUD initiates an investigation.

## Quick Reference: Compliance Checklist for 55+ Communities

- [ ] **Declaration/CC&Rs** contain explicit 55+ age restriction language (not just "adult community")
- [ ] **80% of occupied units** have at least one occupant 55 years or older
- [ ] **Published policies and procedures** demonstrating 55+ intent
- [ ] **Advertising and marketing** consistently references "55 and older" (not just "adult")
- [ ] **Public postings** in common areas describing community as 55+
- [ ] **Age verification procedures** are established and documented
- [ ] **Initial age verification** collected at purchase/lease for each unit
- [ ] **Biennial survey** conducted every two years to verify continued compliance
- [ ] **Survey summaries** maintained and available for inspection
- [ ] **Records retention** policy covers all age verification documents
- [ ] **Consistent enforcement** of age restrictions across all units
- [ ] **Lease provisions** reference 55+ status
- [ ] **Rules enforcement** does not selectively target or exempt based on protected classes other than age
- [ ] **Board and management** are educated on fair housing obligations and HOPA requirements
