If you serve on an HOA or condo board in the Washington, DC metro area, you operate under a legal framework that most volunteer board members have never fully read. That's understandable — the statutes are dense, they differ by jurisdiction, and most board members didn't run for a seat on the board because they wanted to become experts in nonprofit corporate law.

But the consequences of noncompliance are real. Boards that don't follow proper meeting notice requirements risk having their decisions challenged. Boards that mismanage reserve funds face personal liability claims from homeowners. Boards that enforce rules inconsistently open themselves to discrimination complaints under fair housing law.

This guide covers the core statutes that govern HOA and condo associations in DC, Virginia, and Maryland — not as a legal treatise, but as a practical reference for board members who need to know what the law requires and how to stay in compliance.

## Washington, DC: The Condominium Act and Related Statutes

Washington, DC does not have a standalone HOA statute the way Virginia and Maryland do. Most communities in the District that function like HOAs are legally structured as condominium associations.

### DC Condominium Act (DC Code Title 42, Chapter 19)

The Condominium Act is the primary governing statute for condo associations in DC. It covers:

**Formation and structure.** The Act establishes how condominiums are created through a recorded declaration, how common elements are defined, and how the unit owners' association is organized. Every condo in DC exists because a developer followed this Act's formation requirements.

**Board authority and governance.** The board of directors manages the association's affairs. The Act requires boards to hold regular meetings with proper notice to owners. Owners have the right to attend board meetings and, with certain exceptions, to inspect association records (§ 42-1903.09).

**Assessments and budgets (§ 42-1903.13).** The board must adopt an annual budget and levy assessments sufficient to cover operating costs and reserve contributions. Owners must receive notice of the budget and have the opportunity to comment. This is not optional — it's a statutory requirement, and boards that skip the budget-approval process create governance risk.

**Assessment collection and the super-lien (§ 42-1903.13(h)).** DC gives condo associations one of the most powerful collection tools in the country: a "super-lien" that grants the association priority over the first mortgage for up to six months of unpaid assessments. This means that in a foreclosure, the association can recover up to six months of delinquent dues before the mortgage lender collects anything. Few board members realize how significant this provision is — or how to use it properly. Associations that fail to perfect their liens according to the statute's requirements lose this priority.

**Reserve requirements.** The Act requires associations to maintain adequate reserves for major repairs and replacements. While DC does not mandate a reserve study on a specific timeline (unlike Virginia), underfunding reserves exposes the board to claims of fiduciary negligence.

### Horizontal Property Act (DC Code Title 42, Chapter 20)

Older condominiums in DC — those created before the Condominium Act took effect — may still be governed by the Horizontal Property Act. This law is simpler and less detailed, but it still establishes the fundamental framework: individual unit ownership combined with shared common element ownership. Boards governing older DC condos should confirm which Act applies to their community, as the governance requirements differ.

### DC Nonprofit Corporation Act of 2010 (DC Code Title 29, Chapter 4)

Most associations in DC are organized as nonprofit corporations. This Act governs how the corporate entity operates — board elections, meeting procedures, officer duties, recordkeeping, and voting. When your association's bylaws are silent on a procedural question, this Act typically fills the gap. Board members should understand that they are running a corporation, not an informal neighborhood group, and the legal obligations that come with that structure apply.

### DC Human Rights Act

This is the statute most boards overlook. The DC Human Rights Act applies to all aspects of housing in the District — including HOA and condo governance. DC recognizes more than 20 protected classes, significantly more than federal fair housing law. Rule enforcement, access to common areas, architectural review decisions, and any other board action must be applied consistently and without discrimination. Selective enforcement based on any protected characteristic is a violation.

## Virginia: POAA and the Condominium Act

Virginia has two separate governing statutes depending on community type.

### Property Owners' Association Act (POAA) — Va. Code § 55.1-1800 et seq.

The POAA governs HOAs — planned communities, townhouse developments, and single-family subdivisions. Key provisions for boards:

**Disclosure packets (§ 55.1-1809).** When a unit sells, the association must deliver a disclosure packet to the buyer within 14 days of request. The packet includes financial statements, the budget, the reserve study, governing documents, insurance information, and any pending litigation. This is a hard deadline — failure to deliver on time can delay closings and expose the association to liability.

**Reserve study mandate (§ 55.1-1825).** Virginia requires POAA communities to conduct a reserve study at least every five years. The study must identify all major common area components, estimate their remaining useful life, and calculate the funding needed to replace them. This is a statutory obligation, not a suggestion. Boards that skip reserve studies expose themselves to claims of fiduciary negligence — and when a major system fails unexpectedly, they'll face a special assessment and potentially a lawsuit from owners who argue the board should have planned for it.

**Board meetings and elections (§ 55.1-1816, § 55.1-1841).** The POAA requires at least one meeting per year open to all members. Election procedures must comply with the Act's requirements, including secret ballot provisions. Board meetings must follow proper notice requirements — typically 10 to 21 days' written notice depending on the action being taken.

**Assessment collection and liens (§ 55.1-1833).** The POAA authorizes associations to place liens on properties with unpaid assessments and, in some circumstances, to initiate foreclosure. However, Virginia law imposes specific notice requirements before a lien can be perfected. Boards must follow these steps precisely — skipping them can invalidate the lien.

**Architectural review (§ 55.1-1819).** The board has authority to establish and enforce architectural standards, but the review process must follow the governing documents. Decisions must be applied consistently, and denials should include the specific reasons and the governing document provisions that support them.

**Common Interest Community Ombudsman.** Virginia maintains a state-level ombudsman within DPOR that provides resources and mediation assistance for disputes between homeowners and associations. Board members should be aware of this resource because homeowners will use it.

### Virginia Condominium Act — Va. Code § 55.1-1900 et seq.

Condo associations in Virginia operate under the Condominium Act rather than the POAA. The structure is similar but the scope is broader — condo boards are responsible for common elements (structural walls, roofs, elevators, mechanical systems) that POAA boards typically don't manage. The Condominium Act also imposes its own reserve study requirements and disclosure obligations.

Communities in Arlington, Fairfax, Alexandria, and Tysons frequently have large, complex associations — high-rise condos, master-planned communities, mixed-use developments — where compliance with these Acts requires dedicated professional attention.

## Maryland: HOA Act and Condominium Act

Maryland also separates its statutes by community type.

### Maryland Homeowners Association Act — Md. Code, Real Prop. § 11B-101 et seq.

The HOA Act governs planned communities and homeowners associations. Key provisions:

**Annual meetings and transparency (§ 11B-111).** The Act requires an annual meeting and gives homeowners the right to inspect association records, financial statements, and governing documents. Boards that operate without transparency — holding closed meetings, withholding financial information, or making decisions without proper notice — risk both legal challenge and community trust erosion.

**Budget and assessments (§ 11B-112).** The board must prepare and distribute an annual budget. Assessments must be sufficient to fund operations and reserves. Maryland law gives boards the authority to levy assessments, but the process must follow the governing documents — which typically require member notice and, for increases above a certain threshold, a member vote.

**Reserve requirements (§ 11B-112.1).** Maryland requires HOAs to maintain a reserve fund or conduct a reserve study and make the findings available to homeowners. The statute does not prescribe a five-year cycle like Virginia, but Maryland courts and regulators have made clear that boards have a fiduciary duty to plan for major capital expenditures. A board that operates without a reserve study in a community with aging infrastructure is asking for trouble.

**Assessment liens and collection (§ 11B-117).** Associations can place liens on properties for unpaid assessments, but Maryland's lien provisions include specific notice requirements that must be followed precisely. The procedures differ from Virginia and DC, which is why multi-jurisdictional management firms need compliance processes for each.

### Maryland Condominium Act — Md. Code, Real Prop. § 11-101 et seq.

Condo associations in Maryland operate under the Condominium Act, which addresses the broader scope of common element maintenance, unit boundary definitions, and the association's responsibility for structural and mechanical systems. Bethesda and Potomac communities — many of which include aging mid-rise and high-rise condos — frequently deal with reserve shortfalls and capital project planning that require professional management support.

### Maryland Attorney General's Office

Maryland provides dispute resolution resources through the Attorney General's Office. Homeowners who believe their board has violated the HOA Act can file complaints that may result in mediation or investigation. Boards should be aware that this avenue exists and that operating transparently and documenting decisions properly is the best defense against complaints.

## Fiduciary Duty: The Standard That Applies Everywhere

Regardless of jurisdiction, board members in DC, Virginia, and Maryland owe fiduciary duties to their association. These duties include:

- **Duty of care:** Making informed, reasonable decisions. This means reading the financial statements before you vote on the budget, reviewing the reserve study before you approve a capital project, and understanding the governing documents before you enforce a rule.
- **Duty of loyalty:** Acting in the association's interest, not your own. Board members who use their position to benefit personally — steering contracts to friends, waiving their own assessments, or making exceptions to rules for themselves — violate this duty.
- **Duty to act within authority:** The board's power comes from the governing documents and the applicable statutes. Actions that exceed that authority — amending CC&Rs without a proper member vote, imposing fines not authorized by the documents, or spending reserves on operating expenses — are ultra vires and can be challenged by any homeowner.

Fiduciary duty is not theoretical. Homeowners in all three jurisdictions have successfully sued board members for breach of fiduciary duty when boards failed to maintain reserves, selectively enforced rules, or mismanaged association finances.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What law governs HOAs in Washington, DC?**
DC does not have a standalone HOA statute. Most communities that function like HOAs are legally structured as condominium associations governed by the DC Condominium Act (DC Code Title 42, Chapter 19). Associations are also subject to the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act of 2010 and the DC Human Rights Act. For more on board obligations, see our guide to [understanding the legal obligations of HOA boards](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/understanding-the-legal-obligations-of-hoa-boards).

**Does Virginia require a reserve study for HOAs?**
Yes. The Property Owners' Association Act (POAA, § 55.1-1825) requires associations to conduct a reserve study at least every five years. The study must identify major common area components, estimate remaining useful life, and calculate required funding. Boards that skip this obligation risk fiduciary liability. For more on reserves, see our guide on [why every HOA must take reserve funding seriously](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/why-every-hoa-must-take-reserve-funding-seriously).

**Can an HOA foreclose on a homeowner for unpaid dues?**
In all three jurisdictions, associations have lien authority for unpaid assessments — and in some circumstances, foreclosure authority. DC's super-lien provision gives condo associations priority over the first mortgage for up to six months of unpaid dues. Virginia and Maryland also authorize liens and, under certain conditions, foreclosure. However, the notice and procedural requirements differ in each jurisdiction and must be followed precisely.

**What fiduciary duties do board members owe?**
Board members owe a duty of care (making informed decisions), a duty of loyalty (acting in the association's interest, not their own), and a duty to act within the authority granted by the governing documents and applicable law. Breach of these duties can result in personal liability.

**How does the DC super-lien work for condo assessments?**
Under DC Code § 42-1903.13(h), a condo association's lien for unpaid assessments has priority over the first mortgage for up to six months of delinquent dues. This means in a foreclosure, the association can recover those amounts before the mortgage lender. However, the lien must be properly perfected following the statute's requirements. This is one of the strongest assessment collection tools available to any community association in the country.
