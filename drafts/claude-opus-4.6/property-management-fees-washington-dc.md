Understanding what property management costs — and what you get for that cost — is one of the most practical questions a rental property owner can answer before hiring a management company. Yet most property management websites either bury their fee information, list percentages without context, or present pricing so vaguely that owners can't meaningfully compare one firm to another.

This guide breaks down what property management actually costs in Washington, DC, Northern Virginia, and Maryland — with enough detail that you can compare proposals, identify hidden fees, and evaluate whether professional management makes financial sense for your property.

## The Core Management Fee

The management fee is the monthly charge you pay for ongoing property oversight — rent collection, maintenance coordination, tenant communication, financial reporting, compliance management, and general operations.

In the DC metro area, management fees are structured in one of two ways:

### Percentage of Collected Rent

Most firms in the DC metro charge a percentage of monthly rent actually collected. The standard range:

- **Washington, DC:** 8–10% of collected rent
- **Northern Virginia (Arlington, Fairfax, Tysons, Alexandria):** 7–9% of collected rent
- **Maryland (Bethesda, Potomac, Montgomery County):** 7–9% of collected rent

DC tends to run slightly higher than Virginia and Maryland because of the District's regulatory complexity. A manager operating in DC must navigate the Basic Business License (BBL) requirements, rent stabilization filings, TOPA compliance, the Tenant Bill of Rights, source-of-income protections, the Fair Criminal Record Screening Amendment Act, and security deposit rules that carry treble damages for violations. That compliance burden is reflected in pricing.

For a property renting at $2,500/month, an 8% management fee means $200/month, or $2,400/year. At 10%, that's $250/month, or $3,000/year.

### Flat Fee

Some firms charge a fixed monthly amount regardless of rental income. Flat fees in this market typically range from $125 to $300 per month per unit, depending on the property type and services included.

Flat-fee models offer budget predictability, but they can misalign incentives — a flat-fee manager has no financial incentive to push for the highest market rent. Percentage-based firms earn more when your rent is higher, which creates natural alignment.

## Tenant Placement (Leasing) Fees

When your property is vacant and needs a new tenant, the management company handles advertising, showings, applications, screening, and lease execution. This is typically charged as a separate fee.

**Standard range in the DC metro:** 50–100% of one month's rent, or a flat fee of $1,000–$2,500.

Some firms include one placement per year in their management fee. Others charge for every placement. The structure matters significantly because turnover is one of the largest costs a rental property owner faces.

### Why Placement Fee Structure Matters

Turnover in the DC metro area typically costs $3,000–$8,000 per occurrence when you account for:

- Lost rent during vacancy (average 3–6 weeks in this market, depending on season and location)
- Cleaning, painting, and minor repairs between tenants
- Marketing costs for advertising and showing the unit
- Screening costs for processing applications
- The placement fee itself

A firm that charges a high placement fee but averages 30-day vacancy turnovers may cost you more than a firm with a moderate placement fee that consistently turns units in 14–21 days. Ask prospective managers for their average days-on-market for vacant units and their average tenant retention rate — these numbers reveal more than the fee alone.

## Lease Renewal Fees

When an existing tenant's lease expires and both parties agree to renew, some firms charge a renewal fee.

**Standard range:** $0–$300 per renewal.

Many firms have moved away from renewal fees entirely because the math is straightforward: retaining a good tenant is almost always cheaper than finding a new one. A renewal fee can create perverse incentives — a firm that profits from turnover has less motivation to manage proactive renewals.

If a firm charges renewal fees, ask whether they actively manage the renewal process — reaching out to tenants 60–90 days before lease expiration, recommending rent adjustments based on market data, and executing the new lease. A firm that charges $200 for a renewal but adds $50–$100/month in justified rent increases is earning its fee.

## Maintenance Fees and Vendor Markups

How a management company handles maintenance is where fees get opaque — and where owners most often feel surprised.

Three models are common:

### 1. Markup on Vendor Invoices

Some firms add a 10–20% markup on every contractor invoice. A $500 plumbing repair becomes $550–$600. This model is defensible if the firm is handling vendor selection, quality control, and warranty follow-up — but it should be disclosed upfront.

### 2. In-House Maintenance at Cost

Firms with in-house maintenance teams typically charge owners at cost or at a modest internal labor rate, without markup on materials. This model often results in faster response times and lower per-repair costs, but the firm needs a large enough portfolio to justify maintaining staff.

### 3. Coordination Fee Per Work Order

Some firms charge a flat fee ($25–$75) per maintenance request, regardless of the repair cost. This model is less common in the DC metro but exists.

Regardless of model, confirm:

- **What is your approval threshold?** Most management agreements establish a dollar amount (typically $300–$500) below which the manager can authorize repairs without owner approval. Above that threshold, you should be consulted first — except for genuine emergencies.
- **Do you provide competitive bids for larger projects?** For any repair or capital project over $1,000–$2,000, a professional manager should obtain at least two to three bids.
- **Are your vendors licensed and insured?** In DC, contractors need DCRA licenses. In Virginia, they must be registered with DPOR. In Maryland, they need MHIC licenses. Your manager should verify these credentials.

## Vacancy Fees

Some firms charge a reduced monthly fee while your property is vacant — typically 25–50% of the standard management fee.

Others charge nothing during vacancies, arguing that they're incentivized to fill the unit quickly because their own revenue depends on collected rent.

**Our recommendation:** A firm that charges no vacancy fee and operates on a percentage-of-collected-rent model has the strongest alignment with your interests during a vacancy. They don't get paid until you get paid.

## Eviction Fees

In Washington, DC, eviction is not a fast or inexpensive process. Cases filed in DC Superior Court's Landlord-Tenant Branch routinely take 6–12 months from filing to possession, and that timeline assumes no procedural complications.

Most management companies charge a separate fee for managing the eviction process, which includes:

- Serving required notices
- Coordinating with the owner's attorney
- Filing court documents
- Attending hearings
- Coordinating lockout and unit turnover after possession is granted

**Standard range:** $200–$500 for the management company's coordination fee, plus the attorney's legal fees (typically $1,500–$5,000+ depending on complexity).

Virginia and Maryland evictions tend to move faster — Virginia general district court evictions can proceed in 30–60 days in uncontested cases, and Maryland district court has similar timelines. But contested cases in any jurisdiction can extend significantly.

A manager who screens tenants rigorously and manages lease enforcement proactively should help you avoid most eviction scenarios. Ask prospective firms about their eviction rate — a firm that evicts frequently may be screening poorly.

## Early Termination Fees

Most management agreements are 12-month contracts with automatic renewal. If you want to cancel before the term expires, many firms charge an early termination fee.

**Standard range:** $0–3 months of management fees.

A firm that requires 30 days' written notice with no termination fee is offering a reasonable arrangement. A firm that locks you into 12 months with a penalty of two to three months' fees for early exit is asking you to bear significant risk for a service you haven't yet evaluated.

Before signing, read the cancellation clause carefully. Understand:

- How much notice is required?
- Is there a fee, and if so, how is it calculated?
- Are there conditions under which you can terminate without penalty (e.g., cause, sale of property)?

## Setup and Onboarding Fees

Some firms charge a one-time fee when a new property is added to their portfolio. This covers initial inspection, tenant notification, vendor transition, and account setup.

**Standard range:** $0–$500.

Many firms in the DC metro waive this fee to reduce barriers to onboarding. If a firm charges a setup fee, confirm exactly what services are included and whether it covers the initial property inspection and condition report.

## How to Compare Total Annual Cost

Comparing management companies on percentage alone is misleading. A firm charging 8% with a full-month leasing fee, 15% maintenance markup, and a $300 renewal fee may cost significantly more annually than a firm charging 10% with a half-month leasing fee, no maintenance markup, and no renewal fee.

To compare accurately, estimate your total annual cost under each firm's fee schedule:

| Fee Category | Firm A | Firm B |
|---|---|---|
| Management fee (monthly % × 12) | $2,400 | $3,000 |
| Leasing fee (assume one turnover/year) | $2,500 | $1,250 |
| Renewal fee | $200 | $0 |
| Maintenance markup (est. $5K in repairs × markup %) | $750 | $0 |
| **Estimated annual total** | **$5,850** | **$4,250** |

In this example, Firm B charges a higher percentage but costs $1,600 less annually because of lower ancillary fees. Always model the total cost, not just the headline rate.

## What You Should Expect for Your Fees

Regardless of fee level, a professional property management company in the DC metro area should provide:

- **24/7 emergency maintenance coverage** with documented response protocols
- **Online owner portal** with real-time access to financial statements, maintenance logs, lease documents, and inspection reports
- **Monthly owner statements** with clear accounting of rent collected, expenses paid, and net disbursements
- **Annual tax documentation** — 1099s, year-end financial summaries, and support for Schedule E preparation
- **Proactive lease management** — market rent analysis, renewal outreach 60–90 days before expiration, and lease execution
- **Regulatory compliance management** — BBL renewals in DC, DPOR compliance in Virginia, MREC compliance in Maryland, fair housing adherence across all jurisdictions
- **Transparent communication** — same-business-day response to owner inquiries, proactive notification of issues, and regular property condition updates

If a firm cannot clearly articulate what services are included in their fee — or if they hesitate when you ask for a complete fee schedule — that's a signal worth heeding.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much do property managers charge in Washington, DC?**
Monthly management fees in DC typically range from 8% to 10% of collected rent. Leasing fees range from 50% to 100% of one month's rent. DC fees tend to be slightly higher than Virginia and Maryland because of the District's complex regulatory environment — BBL licensing, rent control, TOPA, and enhanced tenant protections all add compliance overhead. For a property renting at $2,500/month, expect $200–$250/month in management fees.

**What is the difference between a management fee and a leasing fee?**
The management fee is your monthly charge for ongoing property oversight — rent collection, maintenance, communication, compliance, and reporting. The leasing fee is a separate, one-time charge assessed when the manager places a new tenant. Not all firms separate these — some include one placement per year in their management fee. Always ask for the complete fee schedule.

**Should I choose a percentage-based or flat-fee manager?**
Percentage-based fees align the manager's revenue with your rental income — they're incentivized to achieve the highest market rent. Flat fees offer budget predictability but can misalign incentives. For most DC metro rental properties, percentage-based arrangements provide better alignment.

**What hidden fees should I watch for?**
The most common surprise fees are maintenance markups (10–20% on vendor invoices), vacancy fees (charged while your property sits empty), lease renewal fees, administrative fees, and early termination penalties. Ask every prospective manager for their complete fee schedule — not just the management percentage.

**How much does tenant turnover actually cost in DC?**
Turnover typically costs $3,000–$8,000 per occurrence in the DC metro area, including lost rent during vacancy (3–6 weeks average), cleaning and repairs, marketing, screening, and the placement fee. This is why tenant retention is one of the most valuable services a property manager provides.
