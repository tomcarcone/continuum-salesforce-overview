A homeowners association is a private organization that governs a residential community. If you've recently joined an HOA board — or you're considering it — the acronym itself is the easy part. The harder part is understanding what the organization actually does, what the board is responsible for, and how the legal framework shapes every decision you make.

This guide is not written for someone deciding whether to buy a home in an HOA community. It's written for the people who run them — current board members, newly elected directors, and homeowners considering a seat on the board. If you serve on a board in Washington, DC, Northern Virginia, or Maryland, this is what you need to understand about the organization you're leading.

## How an HOA Is Structured

An HOA is a legal entity — typically a nonprofit corporation — created by a real estate developer when a community is built. The developer records a set of founding documents that bind every property in the community to the association. When a homeowner purchases a property within the community, they automatically become a member and agree to the association's rules and financial obligations.

The founding documents include:

- **Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs):** The master document. It defines property-use restrictions, architectural standards, maintenance responsibilities, and the association's authority to levy assessments. CC&Rs are recorded with the local land records office and run with the land — they bind every current and future owner.
- **Bylaws:** The association's operational rules. They establish how the board is elected, how meetings are conducted, what constitutes a quorum, how votes are counted, and what officers do.
- **Articles of Incorporation:** The filing that creates the association as a legal entity. Without them, the association cannot own property, enter contracts, or take legal action.
- **Rules and Regulations:** Board-adopted policies that supplement the CC&Rs — covering day-to-day issues like pool hours, parking assignments, pet restrictions, and noise standards.

These documents form a hierarchy. State and federal law supersede everything. Below that, the CC&Rs govern. Bylaws cannot contradict the CC&Rs. Rules cannot contradict the bylaws. Board members who understand this hierarchy avoid the most common governance mistakes.

## HOA vs. Condo Association: A Distinction That Matters

The terms are used interchangeably in casual conversation, but they describe different legal structures with different responsibilities.

**In an HOA** (planned communities, townhouse developments, single-family subdivisions), homeowners own their individual lot and structure. The association owns and maintains shared common areas — streets, sidewalks, parks, pools, clubhouses, landscaping.

**In a condo association**, owners hold title to their individual unit but share ownership of all common elements — structural walls, roofs, lobbies, elevators, hallways, mechanical systems. The association is responsible for maintaining everything outside the unit boundaries.

The distinction matters because the board's maintenance scope is dramatically different. A condo board managing a 200-unit building with elevators, a central HVAC plant, a parking garage, and a roof is operating at a fundamentally different level of complexity than an HOA board managing common landscaping and a community pool.

In DC, most communities that function like HOAs are legally structured as condominium associations under the DC Condominium Act. In Virginia, the Property Owners' Association Act (POAA) governs HOAs while the Virginia Condominium Act governs condos. In Maryland, the Maryland HOA Act and the Maryland Condominium Act serve the same purpose. Board members should know which statute applies to their community — the governance requirements, reserve obligations, and board powers differ.

## What the Board Actually Does

An HOA board of directors is the elected leadership of the association. Board members are volunteer homeowners. They serve without compensation. And they carry real legal obligations.

The board's core responsibilities include:

### Financial Management

The board approves the annual budget, sets assessment amounts, oversees reserve fund contributions, authorizes expenditures, and reviews monthly financial statements. For many associations, this means managing annual budgets that range from $50,000 for a small townhouse community to $2 million or more for a large condo building.

Setting assessments correctly is one of the board's most important — and most politically difficult — functions. Assessments must be high enough to cover operating costs and fund reserves for future capital needs. Boards that keep assessments artificially low to avoid homeowner pushback often create a financial crisis years later when a major system fails and the reserve fund is empty. The alternative — a special assessment — is almost always more painful than a well-planned annual increase.

### Vendor and Contract Management

The board hires and oversees contractors for landscaping, maintenance, cleaning, snow removal, capital projects, and professional services (attorneys, accountants, insurance brokers). In larger communities, vendor management can involve dozens of contracts worth six figures annually.

For communities in Arlington, Fairfax, Alexandria, and Tysons, where labor and material costs run higher than national averages, effective vendor management has a direct impact on the association's financial health.

### Rule Enforcement

The board enforces the CC&Rs and community rules. This includes responding to violations, issuing notices, conducting hearings, and imposing fines when authorized by the governing documents.

Rule enforcement must be consistent. Selective enforcement — allowing one homeowner's violation while penalizing another's — is the fastest route to legal liability. In DC, where the Human Rights Act recognizes more than 20 protected classes, inconsistent enforcement can also trigger discrimination claims.

### Governance and Compliance

The board must conduct meetings with proper notice, hold annual elections, maintain records, and comply with the applicable state statutes. Virginia requires annual meetings open to all members and reserve studies every five years. DC requires budget notice and owner comment opportunities. Maryland requires annual meetings and record access.

Board members who skip procedural requirements risk having their decisions challenged and overturned by homeowners.

### Insurance and Risk Management

The board must maintain adequate insurance — typically property insurance on common elements, general liability insurance, and directors and officers (D&O) coverage. D&O insurance protects individual board members from personal liability for decisions made in good faith. Boards that operate without D&O coverage are asking their volunteers to accept financial risk that is entirely avoidable.

## How HOA Fees Work

HOA fees — assessments, dues — fund two categories: the operating budget and the reserve fund.

**The operating budget** covers recurring annual expenses: landscaping, cleaning, utilities for common areas, management fees, insurance premiums, administrative costs, and routine maintenance.

**The reserve fund** covers future capital expenditures: roof replacement, elevator modernization, parking lot repaving, HVAC system overhaul, pool resurfacing, and other major repairs. A professional reserve study identifies these components, estimates their remaining useful life, and calculates the annual funding needed to replace them without a special assessment.

In the DC metro area, monthly HOA fees range from roughly $100–$200 for small townhouse communities with minimal amenities to $600–$1,200+ for full-service high-rise condos in downtown DC or Bethesda. The amount reflects the community's size, age, amenity level, location, and reserve funding adequacy.

Boards set fees annually based on the approved budget. Under the governing documents, the board typically has authority to increase assessments by a certain percentage (often 10–15%) without a member vote. Increases above that threshold usually require member approval — check your bylaws for the specific provisions.

## When Boards Need Professional Management

Small associations — 10 to 30 units with modest budgets and limited common areas — can sometimes operate effectively with volunteer-only management. But as communities grow, age, or encounter governance challenges, the demands on volunteer board members often exceed what they can reasonably handle.

Signs that professional management has become necessary:

- **Administrative overload.** Board members are spending hours each week on vendor calls, owner inquiries, billing, and recordkeeping instead of focusing on governance decisions.
- **Reserve planning gaps.** The association lacks a current reserve study, the reserve fund is underfunded, and the board isn't sure how to model future capital needs.
- **Compliance uncertainty.** The board isn't confident it's meeting statutory requirements for meetings, notices, disclosures, or financial reporting.
- **Collection problems.** Delinquent assessments are accumulating, and the board doesn't have a systematic collection process that follows jurisdiction-specific lien and notice requirements.
- **Board turnover.** Institutional knowledge is leaving with departing board members, and new directors are starting from scratch.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is an HOA?**
A homeowners association (HOA) is a private governing body that manages a residential community. It is typically organized as a nonprofit corporation, governed by elected homeowner volunteers (the board of directors), and funded through regular assessments paid by all homeowners in the community. The association maintains common areas, enforces community rules, manages finances, and ensures compliance with applicable law.

**What is the difference between an HOA and a condo association?**
In an HOA, homeowners own their lot and structure; the association maintains shared common areas. In a condo association, owners hold title to their unit but share ownership of common elements — structural walls, roofs, elevators, mechanical systems. The board's maintenance responsibilities are broader in a condo association. The governing statutes also differ by jurisdiction. For more, see our [guide to HOA common areas](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/what-exactly-are-hoa-common-areas).

**What are board members' legal obligations?**
Board members owe fiduciary duties to the association: duty of care (making informed decisions), duty of loyalty (acting in the association's interest), and duty to act within the authority granted by the governing documents and applicable law. Breach of fiduciary duty can result in personal liability. For more, see [understanding the legal obligations of HOA boards](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/understanding-the-legal-obligations-of-hoa-boards).

**How much are HOA fees in the DC metro area?**
Monthly fees range from approximately $100–$200 for small townhouse communities to $600–$1,200+ for high-rise condos with full amenities. Fees are set by the board based on the annual budget and must cover both operating expenses and reserve fund contributions. For more on budgeting, see [HOA budgeting best practices for board success](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/hoa-budgeting-best-practices-for-board-success).

**What is a reserve study and does my association need one?**
A reserve study is a professional assessment of an association's major common area components — roofs, HVAC, parking surfaces, elevators, pools — that estimates their remaining useful life and the annual funding needed to replace them. Virginia law requires a reserve study every five years for POAA communities. DC and Maryland do not mandate a specific timeline but treat reserve planning as a core fiduciary obligation. For more, see [why every HOA must take reserve funding seriously](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/why-every-hoa-must-take-reserve-funding-seriously).

**When should an HOA hire a management company?**
Most associations benefit from professional management when they exceed 30–50 units, when administrative demands exceed what volunteers can handle, when reserve planning becomes complex, or when the board faces governance or compliance challenges. Both small and large communities can benefit — the key is matching the management approach to the community's size and needs.
