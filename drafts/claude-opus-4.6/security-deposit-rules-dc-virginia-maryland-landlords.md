Security deposits are among the most regulated aspects of landlord-tenant law — and among the most frequently mishandled. In the DC metro area, where three overlapping jurisdictions each impose different rules on deposit limits, holding requirements, interest obligations, return timelines, and penalty structures, getting deposits wrong is one of the fastest ways for a landlord to turn a routine tenancy into an expensive legal dispute.

This guide covers the specific security deposit rules that apply to landlords in Washington, DC, Virginia, and Maryland, with the practical compliance detail you need to manage deposits correctly from collection through return.

## Security Deposit Limits: How Much Can You Collect?

The maximum security deposit you can charge depends entirely on where your property is located.

### Washington, DC

DC caps security deposits at **one month's rent** (DC Code § 42-3502.17). No exceptions — not for furnished units, not for pets, not for high-end properties. If your unit rents for $2,800/month, the maximum deposit is $2,800.

### Virginia

Virginia allows deposits of up to **two months' rent** under the Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA, Va. Code § 55.1-1226). For a $2,800/month rental, that means you can collect up to $5,600.

### Maryland

Maryland's rules changed on **October 1, 2024**. For leases signed on or after that date, the default maximum is **one month's rent** (Md. Code, Real Prop. § 8-203). The prior two-month cap still applies to leases signed before that date. There is a limited exception allowing up to two months for newer leases when the tenant pays utilities directly to the landlord and qualifies for utility assistance — but this applies in narrow circumstances and requires a written agreement.

If you own rental property in Montgomery County (Bethesda, Potomac), the state rules apply — Montgomery County does not impose a separate deposit cap beyond state law.

## Where and How to Hold Security Deposits

All three jurisdictions require landlords to hold deposits in separate accounts. The details differ.

### Washington, DC

Deposits must be held in an **interest-bearing escrow account at a financial institution located in the District of Columbia**. Within 30 days of collecting the deposit, you must provide the tenant with written notice identifying the bank's name and address. Commingling deposits with personal or operating funds is a violation.

### Virginia

Deposits must be held in a **separate escrow account at a federally insured financial institution**. Virginia does not require the account to be interest-bearing, but it must be separate from the landlord's personal or business funds. If the property is managed by a licensed real estate broker, DPOR regulations require the broker to maintain the escrow account under their brokerage.

### Maryland

Deposits must be held in a **federally insured financial institution in Maryland**. The account must be a separate escrow account. Maryland does not specify that it must be interest-bearing per se — but since Maryland requires interest to be paid to tenants (see below), the practical reality is that the funds must earn interest.

## Interest on Security Deposits

This is where DC metro landlords most frequently make mistakes — especially those who own properties in multiple jurisdictions and apply one set of rules across all of them.

### Washington, DC

DC requires landlords to pay **annual interest** on security deposits at a rate set each year by the DC Department of Housing and Community Development. Interest accrues after the first year of tenancy. You can pay it at each lease anniversary or credit it at move-out — but you must pay it. Failing to do so is a violation of § 42-3502.17.

### Virginia

Virginia **does not require interest** on security deposits. This is a meaningful difference from DC and Maryland. If you own properties in both DC and Virginia, do not assume the Virginia rules apply everywhere.

### Maryland

Maryland requires landlords to pay interest on deposits of **$50 or more** that have been held for at least six months. The rate is the daily U.S. Treasury yield curve rate, but it cannot fall below **1.5% per year** (Md. Code, Real Prop. § 8-203). For leases signed before January 1, 2015, the rate was a flat 3% per year. Interest must be paid annually or credited at move-out, and landlords who fail to pay it can forfeit their right to retain any portion of the deposit.

## Return Timeline and Itemization Requirements

### Washington, DC

You have **45 days after move-out** to return the deposit or provide an itemized statement of deductions. If you are withholding any portion, you must send the itemized statement within **30 days** of the tenancy ending. The statement must describe each deduction with specificity — vague entries like "cleaning" or "damages" without detail are insufficient. If you miss the 45-day deadline or fail to itemize properly, you risk forfeiting the entire deposit and facing **treble (3x) damages**.

That treble damages provision is one of the most consequential penalties in DC landlord-tenant law. On a $2,800 deposit improperly withheld, the potential liability is $8,400 — plus attorney's fees if the tenant prevails in court.

### Virginia

Virginia also provides **45 days** to return the deposit after the tenant vacates and surrenders the keys (Va. Code § 55.1-1226). If you are withholding any portion, you must send a written itemized list of deductions within that period. Virginia does not require you to attach receipts or invoices, but the itemization must be specific enough that the tenant can understand what was deducted and why. Failure to comply entitles the tenant to recover actual damages plus attorney's fees.

Virginia also gives landlords a useful tool: the **move-in inspection report**. Under the VRLTA, landlords must provide a written report of the property's condition within five days of occupancy. Tenants can request to attend the move-out inspection within 72 hours of vacating. If you document the move-in condition thoroughly — with photos, timestamps, and written descriptions — you create a defensible record for any deductions.

### Maryland

Maryland requires return within **45 days** after the lease ends (Md. Code, Real Prop. § 8-203). If deductions are made, the landlord must provide an itemized list of damages and costs, sent via first-class mail to the tenant's last known address. Maryland courts have held landlords to strict compliance with the itemization requirement — vague or incomplete statements can result in forfeiture of the right to retain any deposit.

## What You Can (and Cannot) Deduct

Across all three jurisdictions, allowable deductions generally include:

- **Unpaid rent**
- **Damage beyond normal wear and tear** — broken fixtures, holes in walls, pet damage, stains that cannot be removed with standard cleaning
- **Unauthorized alterations** — modifications the tenant made without written consent
- **Cleaning required to restore the unit to its move-in condition** — but only if the unit was left in a materially worse condition, not for routine turnover cleaning

You **cannot** deduct for:

- **Normal wear and tear** — faded paint, minor scuffs, carpet wear in traffic paths, loose doorknobs from regular use
- **Routine turnover cleaning** — unless the lease specifically allows it and the unit was left in an unreasonably dirty condition
- **Pre-existing conditions** — any damage that was present at move-in and documented in the move-in inspection

The distinction between "damage" and "normal wear and tear" is the single most litigated issue in security deposit disputes. The best protection is a thorough move-in inspection with date-stamped photos and a written condition report signed by the tenant.

## The Five Most Expensive Deposit Mistakes DC Metro Landlords Make

### 1. Missing the 45-Day Deadline

This is the most common and most expensive mistake. Life gets busy, a forwarding address gets lost, and suddenly 46 days have passed. In DC, the penalty is treble damages. In Maryland, you can forfeit the entire deposit. Calendar the return deadline the day the tenant gives notice — not the day they move out.

### 2. Failing to Hold Deposits in Escrow

Landlords who deposit tenant security deposits into their personal checking account are violating the law in all three jurisdictions. Depending on the jurisdiction, commingling can invalidate your right to retain any portion of the deposit, regardless of actual damages.

### 3. Deducting for Normal Wear and Tear

A scuffed baseboard is not damage. Faded paint after a three-year tenancy is not damage. Pin holes from hanging pictures are generally not damage. Landlords who deduct for these items invite disputes they will lose — and in DC, treble damages for wrongful withholding.

### 4. Skipping the Move-In Inspection

Without a documented move-in condition, you have no baseline for proving damage at move-out. Virginia requires a move-in report by statute. DC and Maryland do not mandate it, but landlords who skip it are operating without a safety net. Take date-stamped photos, create a written condition report, and have the tenant sign it.

### 5. Not Paying Interest in DC or Maryland

DC and Maryland both require interest payments on security deposits. Landlords who collect the deposit and forget about interest obligations create a technical violation that tenants (or their attorneys) can exploit — especially if the tenancy ends in a dispute.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the maximum security deposit a landlord can charge in DC?**
Washington, DC limits security deposits to one month's rent, with no exceptions for furnished units or pet deposits (DC Code § 42-3502.17). Virginia allows up to two months' rent. Maryland allows one month's rent for leases signed on or after October 1, 2024. For more on DC landlord requirements, see our [DC landlord licensing guide](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/must-know-rental-licensing-rules-for-landlords).

**How long do DC landlords have to return a security deposit?**
45 days after the tenant moves out. If you're withholding any portion, you must send an itemized deduction statement within 30 days of the tenancy ending. Missing the deadline exposes you to treble (3x) damages under DC Code § 42-3502.17. For more on deposit management best practices, see our [security deposit best practices guide](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/how-to-secure-the-return-of-your-security-deposit).

**Do DC landlords have to pay interest on security deposits?**
Yes. DC requires annual interest at a rate set by the DC Department of Housing and Community Development. Maryland also requires interest at a minimum of 1.5% annually on deposits of $50 or more held for at least six months. Virginia does not require interest.

**Can a landlord deduct for cleaning from a security deposit?**
Only if the unit was left in a materially worse condition than at move-in. Routine turnover cleaning — vacuuming, wiping counters, mopping — is a cost of doing business and is not deductible in most circumstances. Deductions for cleaning require documentation showing the unit's condition at move-in and move-out.

**What happens if a DC landlord wrongfully withholds a security deposit?**
The tenant can file a claim in DC Superior Court and may recover treble (3x) damages plus reasonable attorney's fees. On a $2,800 deposit, that's up to $8,400 in damages alone. This is one of the strongest tenant protections in the country and underscores why proper deposit management is critical.
