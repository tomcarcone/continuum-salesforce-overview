A homeowners association — commonly called an HOA — is a private governing body that manages a residential community. When you buy property in an HOA community, membership is mandatory. You pay regular assessments (often called dues or fees), and in return the association maintains shared spaces, enforces community standards, and handles the operational and financial responsibilities that keep the community functioning.

HOAs are not a minor part of American homeownership. According to the Community Associations Institute, over 75 million Americans live in some form of community association. In the Washington, DC metro area, HOAs and condominium associations are especially prevalent — from high-rise condos in Dupont Circle and Arlington to planned communities in Fairfax County, Bethesda, and Potomac.

But the way an HOA operates — and the laws that govern it — varies significantly depending on whether your community is in DC, Virginia, or Maryland. This guide covers how HOAs work, what board members are responsible for, and the jurisdiction-specific frameworks that shape community governance in the DC metro area.

## How an HOA Works

Every HOA is established through a set of founding legal documents, typically created by the community's developer. These documents include:

- **Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs):** The master document that defines the community's rules, architectural standards, maintenance responsibilities, and assessment obligations. CC&Rs are recorded with the local land records office and run with the land — meaning they bind every current and future owner.
- **Bylaws:** The operational rules for the association itself — how the board is elected, how meetings are conducted, how votes are counted, and how officers serve.
- **Articles of Incorporation:** The document that establishes the HOA as a legal entity, typically a nonprofit corporation.
- **Rules and Regulations:** The board-adopted policies that supplement the CC&Rs — covering everything from pet restrictions to pool hours to parking rules.

When a homeowner purchases a property within the community, they agree to abide by these governing documents and pay the assessments established by the board.

## What HOA Fees Cover

HOA fees — also called assessments or dues — fund the association's operating budget and reserve fund. The operating budget covers day-to-day expenses. The reserve fund covers major repairs and replacements that are planned for the future.

Typical expenses funded by HOA fees include:

- **Common area maintenance:** Landscaping, snow removal, parking lot maintenance, exterior lighting, and cleaning of shared spaces like lobbies, hallways, fitness centers, and pools.
- **Insurance:** The association carries property insurance on common elements and typically carries general liability insurance and directors and officers (D&O) coverage for board members.
- **Utilities:** Water, sewer, trash removal, and electricity for common areas. In some condo communities, certain unit utilities (such as water or heat) may be included in the fee.
- **Management fees:** If the association uses a professional management company, the management fee is paid from the operating budget.
- **Reserve contributions:** A portion of each month's assessment is set aside for future capital expenditures — roof replacement, elevator modernization, repaving, HVAC systems, and other large-ticket items.
- **Administrative costs:** Accounting, legal counsel, annual audits, tax filings, and insurance renewals.

In the DC metro area, monthly HOA fees vary widely depending on community size, amenities, building age, and property type. A small townhouse community in Fairfax with minimal amenities might charge $100–$200 per month. A high-rise condo in downtown DC with a concierge, pool, fitness center, and parking garage might charge $600–$1,200 or more.

The board of directors sets the annual budget and determines the assessment amount. Boards are legally obligated to collect enough in assessments to cover operating costs and fund reserves adequately — underfunding reserves is one of the most common and consequential mistakes volunteer boards make.

## What the HOA Board Does

The HOA board of directors is the elected leadership of the association. Board members are homeowners within the community who volunteer to serve. They are responsible for:

- **Financial oversight:** Approving the annual budget, setting assessment amounts, overseeing reserve fund contributions, authorizing expenditures, and reviewing monthly financial statements.
- **Vendor management:** Hiring and overseeing contractors for landscaping, maintenance, cleaning, and capital projects. In larger communities, this may involve managing six-figure contracts.
- **Rule enforcement:** Enforcing the CC&Rs and community rules consistently and fairly. This includes issuing violation notices, conducting hearings, and imposing fines when necessary.
- **Governance:** Conducting regular board meetings, holding annual meetings, overseeing elections, and ensuring compliance with the association's bylaws and applicable state or local law.
- **Insurance and risk management:** Maintaining adequate insurance coverage, reviewing policies annually, and managing claims.
- **Communication:** Keeping homeowners informed about community decisions, financial status, upcoming projects, and meeting schedules.

Board members serve in a fiduciary capacity — meaning they have a legal duty to act in the best interests of the association, not their own personal interests. This fiduciary duty includes the duty of care (making informed decisions), the duty of loyalty (avoiding conflicts of interest), and the duty to act within the authority granted by the governing documents.

## HOA Law in Washington, DC

Washington, DC does not have a standalone HOA statute. Most communities that function like HOAs in DC are legally structured as condominium associations governed by the **DC Condominium Act (DC Code § 42-1901 et seq.)**.

Key provisions that DC boards and homeowners should understand:

- **Formation and governance:** The DC Condominium Act establishes how condo associations are formed, how common elements are defined, and how the unit owners' association operates.
- **Board authority:** The board of directors manages the affairs of the association. Boards must hold regular meetings and provide homeowners with notice and the opportunity to attend.
- **Budget and assessments (§ 42-1903.13):** The board must prepare an annual budget and levy assessments sufficient to cover operating costs and reserve contributions. Owners must be given notice of the budget and have the opportunity to comment.
- **Reserve requirements:** DC law requires condo associations to maintain reserve funds for major repairs and replacements. A professional reserve study — while not mandated by a specific statutory deadline the way Virginia requires it — is strongly recommended and considered a governance best practice.
- **Assessment liens (§ 42-1903.13(h)):** DC condo associations have a powerful collection tool — a "super lien" that grants the association priority over the first mortgage for up to six months of unpaid assessments. This is one of the strongest assessment collection provisions in the country.
- **Meeting and records access (§ 42-1903.09):** Homeowners have the right to attend board meetings and inspect association records, with certain exceptions for privileged or confidential matters.
- **Dispute resolution:** The DC Condominium Act provides for mediation and arbitration of disputes between homeowners and the association.

DC boards should also be aware that the **DC Human Rights Act** applies to HOA and condo governance — meaning the association cannot discriminate in rule enforcement, access to common areas, or any other action based on the more than 20 protected classes recognized under DC law.

## HOA Law in Virginia

Virginia has two primary statutes governing community associations:

- **Property Owners' Association Act (POAA) — Va. Code § 55.1-1800 et seq.:** Governs HOAs (planned communities, townhouse communities, single-family subdivisions).
- **Virginia Condominium Act — Va. Code § 55.1-1900 et seq.:** Governs condo associations.

Key provisions for Virginia boards:

- **Disclosure requirements:** Virginia requires sellers to provide a disclosure packet to prospective buyers that includes the association's financial statements, budget, reserve study, rules, and any pending litigation. The association must deliver this packet within 14 days of request.
- **Reserve study mandate (§ 55.1-1825):** The POAA requires associations to conduct a reserve study at least once every five years. This study must identify all major common area components, estimate their remaining useful life, and calculate the funding needed to replace them. Boards that skip reserve studies expose themselves to claims of fiduciary negligence.
- **Board meetings and elections (§ 55.1-1816, § 55.1-1841):** The POAA requires boards to hold at least one meeting per year open to all members. Election procedures must comply with the Act's requirements, including provisions for secret ballots in certain elections.
- **Assessment collection and liens (§ 55.1-1833):** The POAA grants associations the authority to place liens on properties with unpaid assessments and, in some circumstances, to initiate foreclosure proceedings.
- **Architectural control (§ 55.1-1819):** Boards have the authority to establish and enforce architectural standards, but modifications must be reviewed in accordance with the governing documents and applied consistently.
- **Common Interest Community Ombudsman:** Virginia has a state-level ombudsman (within DPOR) who provides resources and mediation assistance for disputes between homeowners and associations.

Virginia communities in Arlington, Fairfax, Alexandria, and Tysons often have large, complex associations with significant common area infrastructure. Professional management is especially important in these communities, where reserve obligations, vendor oversight, and regulatory compliance can overwhelm volunteer boards.

## HOA Law in Maryland

Maryland also has separate statutes for HOAs and condos:

- **Maryland Homeowners Association Act — Md. Code, Real Prop. § 11B-101 et seq.:** Governs HOAs.
- **Maryland Condominium Act — Md. Code, Real Prop. § 11-101 et seq.:** Governs condo associations.

Key provisions for Maryland boards:

- **Annual meetings and homeowner rights (§ 11B-111):** The HOA Act requires associations to hold an annual meeting and provide homeowners with access to association records, financial statements, and governing documents.
- **Budget and assessments (§ 11B-112):** The board must prepare and distribute an annual budget. Assessments must be sufficient to fund operations and reserves.
- **Reserve requirements (§ 11B-112.1):** Maryland law requires HOAs to maintain a reserve fund or, alternatively, to obtain a reserve study and make the findings available to homeowners. Boards should treat reserve funding as a core fiduciary obligation — not an optional line item.
- **Assessment liens and collection (§ 11B-117):** Associations can place liens on properties for unpaid assessments. Maryland's lien provisions include notice requirements that must be followed precisely.
- **Dispute resolution:** Maryland provides for mediation through the Attorney General's Office for disputes between homeowners and associations.

In Montgomery County and Prince George's County — the Maryland jurisdictions most relevant to the DC metro market — communities often deal with aging building infrastructure, high reserve needs, and complex regulatory environments. Bethesda and Potomac communities in particular tend to have premium amenities and higher assessment levels, requiring sophisticated financial management from their boards.

## HOA vs. Condo Association: What's the Difference?

The terms are often used interchangeably, but they describe different legal structures:

- **HOA (Homeowners Association):** Typically governs planned communities, townhouse developments, and single-family subdivisions. Homeowners own their individual lots and structures outright. The association owns and maintains common areas — roads, parks, pools, landscaping.
- **Condominium Association:** Governs condo buildings and communities. Owners hold title to their individual units but share ownership of common elements — structural walls, roofs, lobbies, elevators, mechanical systems. The association is responsible for maintaining all common elements.

The distinction matters because the governing statutes differ (POAA vs. Condominium Act in Virginia, HOA Act vs. Condo Act in Maryland), and the maintenance responsibilities are divided differently. In a condo association, the board is typically responsible for a much larger scope of physical infrastructure than in a typical HOA.

## When Should an HOA Consider Professional Management?

Many smaller HOAs operate with volunteer-only boards. But as communities grow — or as regulatory, financial, and operational demands increase — the burden on volunteer board members can become unsustainable.

Signs that an association may benefit from professional management include:

- The community has **50 or more units** and the administrative workload exceeds what volunteers can handle
- The board struggles with **reserve planning, budgeting, or financial reporting**
- **Vendor management** — landscaping, maintenance, capital projects — consumes significant board time
- The community has experienced **compliance issues, legal disputes, or collection problems**
- Board members feel they lack the expertise to handle **jurisdiction-specific legal requirements**
- **Turnover on the board** is high, and institutional knowledge is being lost

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is an HOA and how does it work?**
An HOA is a private governing body that manages a residential community. Membership is mandatory for all property owners within the community. Members pay regular assessments that fund common area maintenance, insurance, reserves, and administrative costs. An elected board of directors oversees the association's finances, vendors, rule enforcement, and governance.

**Are HOAs required by law in DC, Virginia, or Maryland?**
HOAs are not required by law — they are created by developers through recorded covenants. However, once established, the governing documents are legally binding on all current and future owners. The applicable statutes (DC Condominium Act, Virginia POAA, Maryland HOA Act) regulate how associations must operate.

**How much are HOA fees in the DC metro area?**
Fees vary widely. Townhouse communities with minimal amenities may charge $100–$200/month. Mid-rise condos in Arlington or Bethesda typically charge $300–$600/month. High-rise condos in downtown DC with full amenities can charge $600–$1,200+ per month. Fees are set by the board based on the annual budget. For more, see our guide to [HOA budgeting best practices](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/hoa-budgeting-best-practices-for-board-success).

**What is a reserve study and does my HOA need one?**
A reserve study is a professional assessment of an association's major common area components — roofs, HVAC, parking surfaces, elevators, pools — that estimates their remaining useful life and the funding needed to replace them. Virginia law (POAA § 55.1-1825) requires a reserve study at least every five years. DC and Maryland do not mandate a specific timeline but strongly encourage reserve studies as a governance best practice. For more, see [Why Every HOA Needs a Reserve Study](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/why-every-hoa-needs-a-reserve-study).

**What is the difference between an HOA and a condo association?**
In an HOA, homeowners own their lot and structure; the association owns and maintains shared common areas. In a condo association, owners hold title to their individual unit but share ownership of all common elements — structural walls, roofs, lobbies, elevators, and mechanical systems. The governing statutes differ (POAA vs. Condo Act), and the board's maintenance responsibilities are typically broader in a condo association.

**What should I do if my HOA board is not maintaining common areas?**
Start by raising the issue formally at a board meeting or in writing. If the board does not respond, review your governing documents for dispute resolution procedures, contact the Virginia Common Interest Community Ombudsman (for VA communities), or consult a community association attorney. For more on board obligations, see [Understanding the Legal Obligations of HOA Boards](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/understanding-the-legal-obligations-of-hoa-boards).
