An HOA is not just a set of rules. It is an operating organization.

That distinction matters because many board members inherit their role after living in the community for years, but they still have never received a simple, practical explanation of what the association actually is, what it controls, and where its authority comes from.

If you serve on a board in Washington, DC, Virginia, or Maryland, understanding the definition of an HOA is the first step to making better decisions about budgeting, governance, resident communication, maintenance, and professional management.

## What Is an HOA?

An HOA, or **homeowners association**, is a private organization created to govern, maintain, and administer a residential community.

At a basic level, an HOA exists to do three things:

- manage shared obligations the individual owners cannot handle alone
- collect money to fund those obligations
- enforce the rules and standards that keep the community functioning

That can include anything from landscaping and snow removal to reserve planning, building maintenance, parking administration, amenity management, insurance coordination, and vendor oversight.

In many communities, owners use "HOA" as a catch-all term. Legally, however, not every association is structured the same way.

## HOA vs. Condo Association: Why the Distinction Matters

In everyday conversation, people often use "HOA" to describe every kind of community association. But for boards, the difference between an HOA and a condominium association matters.

In a traditional HOA:

- owners typically own their home and lot directly
- the association usually maintains shared common areas such as roads, entry features, clubhouse spaces, green space, or amenities

In a condominium association:

- owners usually own the interior of their unit
- the association is typically responsible for more of the building envelope and common systems, including shared structural, mechanical, or interior common-area elements

That difference affects budgets, reserve needs, insurance structure, maintenance responsibility, and risk.

For example, a 30-unit condo association in Washington, DC often has a very different operational burden than a suburban HOA in Fairfax or a townhome association in Montgomery County.

## What an HOA Board Actually Does

A board is not there just to approve meeting minutes and respond to complaints.

The board is the governing body that sets direction for the community. Depending on the size and type of association, the board is responsible for:

- adopting the annual budget
- setting assessment levels
- approving contracts
- overseeing reserve planning
- making policy decisions
- enforcing governing documents
- supervising the management company, if there is one
- communicating major decisions to owners

That means even a volunteer board is operating something much closer to a small business or nonprofit corporation than many members initially realize.

## Where the HOA Gets Its Authority

An HOA does not create power for itself. Its authority comes from a combination of:

- recorded governing documents
- applicable state or District statutes
- corporate structure and board process

The governing documents usually include a declaration, bylaws, and rules or resolutions. These set the specific standards the community lives under.

The statutes matter because they shape how the association exists and what the board is allowed to do.

## How the Answer Changes in DC, Virginia, and Maryland

This is where national HOA articles usually stop being helpful.

### Washington, DC

In the District, many communities that people casually call HOAs are actually structured as condominium associations. That means the legal framework often runs through the **DC Condominium Act**, with support from the **Horizontal Property Act** for some older communities and the **DC Nonprofit Corporation Act** for governance mechanics.

For board members in DC, understanding the association means understanding that the board is often managing a building-centric operation with heavier records, budgeting, and common-element obligations than a typical suburban HOA.

### Virginia

Virginia separates its association laws more clearly.

- Traditional HOAs are commonly governed by the **Property Owners' Association Act**
- Condominium communities are governed by the **Virginia Condominium Act**

For boards in Arlington, Fairfax, Tysons, and Alexandria, that distinction affects reserves, disclosure requirements, elections, and owner rights. Communities with larger amenities, structured parking, elevators, or mixed-use characteristics often carry much more operational complexity than the term "HOA" suggests.

### Maryland

Maryland also separates the framework by community type.

- Traditional HOAs typically operate under the **Maryland Homeowners Association Act**
- Condominiums operate under the **Maryland Condominium Act**

Boards in Bethesda, Potomac, and surrounding markets often have to navigate aging infrastructure, reserve pressure, vendor management, and owner expectations that feel more institutional than informal.

## What HOA Fees Are Really For

Residents often experience HOA fees as a bill. Boards have to treat them as an operating tool.

Assessments are what allow the association to:

- maintain common areas
- pay insurance and utilities
- fund management and accounting support
- build reserves for major repairs
- respond to emergencies
- keep the association legally and financially stable

When boards under-collect to avoid owner frustration, the short-term relief often turns into long-term instability. Dues that are too low today usually become deferred maintenance or special assessments tomorrow.

## HOA Rules Are Not the Same as Good Governance

Owners often think the association is mostly about rules. Rules matter, but that is only one part of the picture.

Healthy associations also depend on:

- clear meeting process
- timely owner communication
- organized records
- fair enforcement
- realistic reserve planning
- well-supervised vendors

Boards that focus only on violations and overlook governance fundamentals usually create conflict without solving the deeper operational problems.

## A Better Definition for Board Members

If you serve on a board, the best definition of an HOA is not just "an organization that enforces rules."

It is this:

**An HOA or community association is the structure owners use to govern shared property, fund shared obligations, and make collective decisions that protect the long-term health of the community.**

That definition is more useful because it matches the actual job of the board.

## FAQ

**What does HOA stand for?**  
HOA stands for homeowners association. It is the private organization that governs and administers a residential community.

**Is an HOA the same as a condo association?**  
Not exactly. People often use the terms loosely, but the ownership structure and maintenance responsibilities are different. For boards, that difference affects budgets, insurance, and reserve planning.

**Why do HOA fees exist?**  
They fund the shared costs the association is responsible for, including maintenance, insurance, management, utilities, reserves, and other operational obligations.

**What is the main job of an HOA board?**  
The board governs the association. That includes budgeting, policy decisions, contract oversight, reserve planning, enforcement, and supervision of management partners.

**When should a board hire a management company?**  
Usually when volunteer administration is no longer enough to keep finances, communication, meetings, vendors, and owner expectations organized and consistent.
