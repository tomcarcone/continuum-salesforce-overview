Most volunteer board members do not start serving because they want to study statutory law. They join because they care about their building, their neighbors, and the long-term health of the community.

The problem is that in Washington, DC, association decisions are rarely just operational. They are also legal.

Whether your board is approving a budget, responding to a records request, planning a major repair, or enforcing a rule, your authority comes from a mix of statute, governing documents, and corporate process. If you understand only one of those pieces, you are operating with a blind spot.

This guide explains the parts of DC association law that matter most in day-to-day board work.

## First, Understand the Structure: DC Does Not Operate Like a Typical HOA State

One of the biggest sources of confusion is that Washington, DC does not have the same kind of standalone HOA statute many board members expect.

In the District, many communities that function like HOAs are legally structured as **condominium associations**. That means the primary legal framework is often not an "HOA act" at all. It is the **DC Condominium Act**, supported by the **Horizontal Property Act** for some older communities and the **DC Nonprofit Corporation Act** for corporate governance issues.

That distinction matters because boards sometimes search for generic HOA rules and miss the statutes that actually govern their association.

## The DC Condominium Act Is the Main Operating Framework

For most modern DC condo communities, the DC Condominium Act is the core statute.

From a board perspective, the law matters because it supports the association's power to:

- maintain and repair common elements
- levy and collect assessments
- enter into contracts
- adopt and enforce rules
- manage association records
- act through the board on behalf of the community

That sounds straightforward, but in practice it affects almost every board meeting agenda.

If your board is debating a roof replacement, elevator contract, reserve contribution, delinquent owner account, or common-area rule, you are operating inside this framework whether you realize it or not.

## Older Buildings May Still Touch the Horizontal Property Act

Many DC associations are in older buildings, and that creates an extra layer of complexity.

Some communities were created under the **Horizontal Property Act**, which predates the modern Condominium Act. Boards of older properties should confirm which legal framework applies to their building and where the governing documents may still reflect older language or older assumptions.

That is especially important when the board is reviewing ownership interests, maintenance responsibilities, or amendment language that does not read the way a newer condominium declaration would.

## The Nonprofit Corporation Act Matters More Than Many Boards Realize

Association boards often think of themselves as governing a building. Legally, they are also running a corporation.

That is where the **DC Nonprofit Corporation Act of 2010** becomes important.

When your bylaws or declaration do not answer a procedural question, this Act often fills the gap. It influences:

- board elections
- officer roles
- meeting procedures
- notice requirements
- recordkeeping
- voting mechanics
- corporate authority

If your board has ever asked, "Can we do that by email?" or "Do we need a formal vote?" or "What if the bylaws are silent?" you were already in Nonprofit Act territory.

## Your Governing Documents Still Drive the Day-to-Day

State and District law are not the whole picture.

In real life, most board actions flow through the association's own governing documents:

- declaration or master deed
- bylaws
- rules and regulations
- resolutions and policies

These documents are where boards typically find the real operating detail on:

- assessment authority
- maintenance boundaries
- architectural review
- use restrictions
- board election structure
- owner obligations
- enforcement procedures

The legal mistake many boards make is treating governing documents as separate from statutory law. They are not separate. They work together.

A strong board asks two questions before making a decision:

1. What do our documents authorize?
2. What does District law require or limit?

## Five Areas Where DC Boards Most Often Create Risk

### 1. Budgeting Without a Long-Term Plan

Boards usually know they need an annual budget. What they underestimate is how quickly underfunded reserves turn into governance problems.

In DC, many condo buildings are older, more expensive to repair, and more mechanically complex than volunteer boards expect. If the board keeps dues artificially low and delays reserve funding, major repairs eventually show up as special assessments, deferred maintenance, or resident distrust.

Budgeting is not just about this year's operating costs. It is about whether the association is preparing responsibly for roofs, facades, elevators, mechanical systems, and major common-area components.

For a deeper planning resource, see [Why Every HOA Must Take Reserve Funding Seriously](https://www.gordonjamesrealty.com/resources/knowledge-hub/why-every-hoa-must-take-reserve-funding-seriously).

### 2. Treating Meetings Informally

Boards that move too casually on meetings, minutes, and notice often create avoidable conflict.

Even when a board is acting in good faith, poor process can undermine good decisions. Owners become skeptical when they do not understand how a vote happened, what was approved, or whether the board followed procedure.

That is why meeting preparation, agenda discipline, recorded votes, and consistent minutes matter so much.

### 3. Weak Records Practices

Owners expect access, transparency, and consistency. Boards that cannot produce contracts, prior minutes, insurance information, or budget records quickly often look disorganized even when they are trying to act responsibly.

More importantly, incomplete records make transitions harder when a board changes, when a manager turns over, or when a dispute arises.

Good recordkeeping is one of the simplest risk-reduction tools a board has.

### 4. Inconsistent Rule Enforcement

Selective enforcement is where operational frustration can turn into legal exposure.

If one owner is cited and another is ignored for the same issue, the board loses credibility fast. That applies to pets, move-in procedures, architectural requests, common-area use, and nuisance complaints.

Boards do not need to be harsh to be effective, but they do need to be consistent, documented, and tied to the actual governing documents.

### 5. Taking on Complex Projects Without Management Support

Small associations especially can fall into the habit of trying to self-manage everything:

- contract bidding
- insurance renewals
- reserve planning
- collections
- vendor supervision
- major repair coordination

That may work for a time, but complexity compounds quickly. Even a 10-unit building can become operationally heavy if the board is dealing with façade issues, waterproofing, delinquency, and owner communication at the same time.

## What DC Boards Should Ask Themselves Every Year

At least once a year, a board should step back and ask:

- Are our governing documents still aligned with how the building actually operates?
- Are our reserves realistic for the systems we maintain?
- Are our meetings and minutes consistent enough to stand up to owner scrutiny?
- Do we have a reliable records system?
- Are we enforcing rules evenly?
- Are we expecting too much volunteer labor from the board?

These questions matter whether your community has 12 units or 300-plus.

## FAQ

**What law governs HOAs in Washington, DC?**  
For many DC communities, the main governing statute is the DC Condominium Act rather than a standalone HOA act. Older communities may also still intersect with the Horizontal Property Act, and most associations are also affected by the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act.

**Why does the Nonprofit Corporation Act matter to a condo board?**  
Because the association is typically organized as a nonprofit corporation. That means elections, meetings, officer roles, and certain governance procedures are shaped not only by the condo documents but also by corporate law.

**Do governing documents matter as much as DC statutes?**  
Yes. In practice, boards need both. The statute provides the legal framework, but the declaration, bylaws, and rules often control the operational details of how the community functions.

**What is the biggest legal risk for a volunteer board?**  
Usually it is not one single law. It is a pattern of weak process: underfunded reserves, poor records, inconsistent enforcement, and informal decision-making that creates owner conflict.

**When should a board involve professional management or counsel?**  
Boards should get help when they are handling significant capital work, persistent delinquency, document interpretation issues, owner disputes, elections, or repeated compliance questions they cannot confidently resolve on their own.
