Security deposits are straightforward in concept: a landlord collects money at the start of a tenancy as financial protection against damage or unpaid rent, and returns it at the end if the tenant meets their obligations. In practice, this is where some of the most common and most expensive landlord compliance mistakes happen.

In the DC metro area, security deposit law is not one law — it's three, each with different caps, different timelines, different interest requirements, and different penalty structures for noncompliance. Getting this wrong doesn't just cost you the deposit. In some jurisdictions, a landlord who mishandles a security deposit can end up paying the tenant damages that far exceed the original amount collected.

Here's what the law actually requires, jurisdiction by jurisdiction.

## Washington DC Security Deposit Rules

DC applies some of the strictest security deposit regulations in the country. Every landlord renting residential property in DC should understand the following before collecting a deposit from any tenant.

### Deposit Limit

Under 14 DCMR § 308.1, a landlord cannot charge a security deposit that exceeds the equivalent of one month's rent. This is a hard cap, and it applies regardless of the tenant's credit profile, the property type, or any other factor. You cannot collect a higher deposit in exchange for waiving a fee or otherwise reframe the amount — the cap is on total security deposit collected.

### Interest Requirements

DC requires landlords to keep security deposits in an escrow account and to pay interest on deposits when the tenancy is renewed or extended beyond one year. The interest rate is set annually by the DC government. This is a landlord obligation, not an optional arrangement — failure to pay interest is a violation.

### Return Timeline

After a tenant vacates, a DC landlord has 45 days to return the security deposit or provide a written, itemized statement of deductions along with any remaining balance. This 45-day window begins from the date the tenancy ends — typically the lease expiration date or the date the tenant actually vacates, whichever is later.

The itemized statement must describe each deduction specifically. "Cleaning fees" or "repairs" without specific descriptions and supporting documentation do not meet DC's standard. Attach receipts or invoices where available.

### Allowable Deductions

Landlords may deduct from the security deposit for:
- Unpaid rent
- Damage beyond normal wear and tear
- Costs to restore the property to its move-in condition, beyond normal use

You cannot deduct for normal wear and tear — routine deterioration from ordinary use of the property. In DC, this standard is applied consistently, and courts and adjudicators have a broad understanding of what qualifies. When in doubt, the deduction probably doesn't hold.

To support deductions, document the property's condition at move-in and move-out with written checklists and date-stamped photographs. A move-in inspection signed by the tenant creates the baseline. Without it, any deduction dispute becomes your word against theirs.

### Penalty for Noncompliance

A landlord who fails to return the deposit or provide a proper itemized statement within 45 days may forfeit the right to retain any portion of the deposit and may be liable for the full deposit amount plus additional damages. DC's Office of the Tenant Advocate (OTA) and the Rental Accommodations Division handle security deposit complaints, and DC courts have historically viewed security deposit violations seriously.

### Pet Deposits in DC

DC landlords may collect a separate pet deposit. However, the total of all deposits combined — security deposit plus any pet deposit — cannot exceed one month's rent under DC's deposit cap. A landlord cannot collect a one-month security deposit and an additional pet deposit on top; the total amount must stay within the cap.

Note: DC law also prohibits discrimination against tenants with assistance animals (service animals and emotional support animals under the DC Human Rights Act). Assistance animals are not pets, and landlords cannot collect a pet deposit for a tenant's assistance animal.

## Virginia Security Deposit Rules (VRLTA)

Virginia's security deposit law is governed by the Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA), codified at Virginia Code § 55.1-1226. Virginia's rules are more permissive than DC's in terms of the deposit cap but carry their own compliance requirements landlords must follow.

### Deposit Limit

Under VRLTA § 55.1-1226, a landlord cannot collect a security deposit exceeding two months' rent. This includes all deposits taken in connection with the tenancy — if you collect a pet deposit in addition to a security deposit, the total cannot exceed two months' rent.

### Return Timeline

A Virginia landlord must return the security deposit — along with any interest owed and a written, itemized statement of deductions — within 45 days after the tenancy ends and the tenant has vacated the property.

### Interest Requirements

Virginia requires landlords to pay interest on security deposits held for more than 13 months. The interest rate is set annually by statute. This obligation applies even if the tenant doesn't specifically request it.

### Allowable Deductions

Virginia allows deductions for:
- Rent owed
- Damages beyond normal wear and tear
- Costs of noncompliance with the lease, including early termination fees where applicable

Virginia, like DC, draws a firm line between actual damage and normal wear and tear. Common items courts have considered normal wear and tear in Virginia include minor scuffs on walls, worn carpet in high-traffic areas, and faded paint from sunlight exposure. Damage from neglect, pets, or tenant alterations is different.

Move-in inspection reports are critically important in Virginia as well. Under VRLTA § 55.1-1214, landlords must offer tenants a move-in inspection checklist. If a landlord fails to do so, they may be limited in what deductions they can make from the deposit.

### Penalty for Noncompliance

A Virginia landlord who wrongfully withholds any portion of a security deposit may be liable for actual damages plus the security deposit, and the court may award attorney's fees to the tenant. Landlords who fail to return the deposit entirely within the 45-day window lose the right to withhold any portion of it.

### Locality Notes for Northern Virginia

Northern Virginia landlords should be aware that VRLTA applies uniformly across the state — county-level variations don't change the core deposit rules. However, property type matters. Single-family homes in Virginia can, under certain circumstances, be exempt from VRLTA if the owner is managing individually (rather than through a property manager) and both parties agree in writing. When a licensed property manager is managing the property, VRLTA applies in full.

## Maryland Security Deposit Rules

Maryland's security deposit law is found in Maryland Real Property § 8-203 through § 8-213. Maryland is the strictest of the three jurisdictions when it comes to deduction documentation requirements and penalties for violations.

### Deposit Limit

Under Maryland Real Property § 8-203, a landlord cannot collect a security deposit exceeding two months' rent. The cap applies to all deposits combined.

### Return Timeline

Maryland landlords must return the security deposit — with any accrued interest and an itemized list of deductions — within 45 days after the tenancy ends.

### Interest Requirements

Maryland requires landlords who hold a security deposit for six months or longer to pay interest at 1.5% per year, simple interest. This interest must be paid when the deposit is returned — not upon request. The obligation is automatic.

Unlike DC and Virginia, Maryland does not require landlords to hold deposits in a dedicated escrow account, but many experienced landlords do so as a matter of best practice and documentation.

### Allowable Deductions and Documentation

Maryland allows deductions for unpaid rent and damages beyond normal wear and tear. What distinguishes Maryland is the documentation standard: a landlord who withholds any portion of a deposit must provide a written statement listing each item of damage and its cost, along with receipts or estimates for the work. Under § 8-211, if a landlord provides documentation within 15 days of the tenancy ending (and the full statement within 45 days), they can protect their right to make deductions. If they miss the 15-day documentation window, the right to certain deductions may be compromised.

This two-step documentation process is not intuitive for landlords coming from other markets. The practical approach: conduct and document the move-out inspection immediately when the tenant vacates, order repairs promptly, and get written estimates in hand within 15 days even if the work isn't complete.

### Penalty for Noncompliance

Maryland has the most significant penalty structure of the three jurisdictions. Under § 8-211, a landlord who wrongfully withholds any portion of a security deposit can be required to pay the tenant up to three times the amount wrongfully withheld, plus reasonable attorney's fees. Maryland courts take security deposit violations seriously, and the triple-damages penalty is applied when a landlord's withholding is found to be without factual basis.

### Montgomery County and Bethesda/Potomac Market Note

Montgomery County landlords should be aware that the county may apply additional licensing requirements (specifically the Montgomery County Landlord-Tenant Relations Ordinance and rental registration requirements through the Department of Permitting Services). These requirements run parallel to Maryland state law and don't supersede the state security deposit rules, but they add an additional compliance layer that DC metro landlords managing property in Bethesda, Potomac, or other Montgomery County locations need to account for.

## The Most Common Security Deposit Mistakes Landlords Make

Across all three jurisdictions, these are the situations that most often lead to security deposit disputes:

**No move-in inspection documentation.** Without a signed move-in inspection or photographic evidence of the property's condition at the start of a tenancy, any deduction claim at move-out is vulnerable. Document condition at move-in with a written checklist and photographs, and have the tenant sign the checklist.

**Deducting for normal wear and tear.** Paint that has faded, carpet that has worn from normal foot traffic, minor scuffs on walls — courts and hearing officers in all three jurisdictions treat these as normal wear and tear, not tenant damage. Deducting for these items creates both a legal vulnerability and a tenant dispute.

**Missing the return deadline.** The 45-day window is firm in all three jurisdictions. Missing it — even by a few days — can forfeit your right to retain any deductions and expose you to additional liability.

**Vague itemization.** An itemized statement that says "repairs: $450" doesn't meet the legal standard in any of these jurisdictions. Each deduction needs to be described specifically, with supporting documentation.

**Collecting more than the legal cap.** In DC especially, where the cap is one month's rent, landlords sometimes collect a deposit and a separate pet deposit without realizing that the combined amount exceeds the limit. Know the cap in each jurisdiction where you own property.

**Not accounting for interest.** Both Virginia and Maryland require interest payments on deposits held past certain timeframes. DC requires interest when deposits are renewed. Forgetting this obligation is a compliance violation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I charge a higher security deposit for tenants with pets in DC?**  
No. In DC, the total of all deposits — including any pet deposit — cannot exceed one month's rent. You can collect a pet deposit, but it must come within that overall cap, not on top of a full security deposit.

**What counts as "normal wear and tear" in DC and Virginia?**  
Normal wear and tear generally means deterioration from ordinary, expected use of a rental property over the term of the tenancy. Examples: minor wall scuffs, carpet wear from regular foot traffic, small nail holes from hanging pictures. Damage from neglect, pet activity, accidents, or tenant modifications is a separate category and can support a deduction if properly documented.

**Do I have to pay interest on a DC security deposit?**  
Yes. DC law requires landlords to maintain deposits in an escrow account and pay interest when tenancies are renewed or extended beyond one year. The annual rate is set by the DC government. Failure to pay interest is a violation of DC's security deposit rules.

**What happens if a tenant disputes my deductions?**  
In DC and Maryland, tenants can file complaints with municipal agencies (the DC Office of the Tenant Advocate and the Maryland Office of the Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division, respectively). Virginia tenants can pursue claims in General District Court. In all three jurisdictions, landlords who cannot substantiate deductions with documentation are in a weak position. The best protection is thorough move-in and move-out documentation and prompt, itemized processing.

**As a landlord with properties in multiple jurisdictions, how do I keep the rules straight?**  
The practical answer for most owners managing multiple properties across DC, Virginia, and Maryland is to work with a property manager who understands the specific rules in each jurisdiction and applies them consistently. The rules are different enough that a one-size-fits-all approach creates compliance risk. If you're managing your own properties across multiple jurisdictions, consider building jurisdiction-specific checklists for deposit collection, documentation, and return procedures.
