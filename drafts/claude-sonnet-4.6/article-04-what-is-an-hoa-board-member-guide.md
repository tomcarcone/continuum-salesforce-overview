Most explanations of "what is an HOA" are written for people considering buying in one — they cover the basics of dues, rules, and shared amenities, and they're aimed at helping prospective residents decide whether community association living is right for them.

This isn't that article.

If you're on the board of an HOA or condo association in Washington DC, Northern Virginia, or Maryland, the more relevant question isn't what it means to live in one — it's what it means to *run* one. The board is the governing body of a legal entity with real obligations, real authority, and real liability if it's exercised carelessly. Understanding what your HOA actually is — legally and operationally — is the foundation of everything else.

## What an HOA Actually Is — Starting with the Structure

A homeowners association is a legal entity, typically formed as a nonprofit corporation, responsible for the governance and management of a planned residential community. The HOA owns and maintains common areas and amenities, enforces the community's governing rules, collects assessments from members, and acts on behalf of the community in legal and financial matters.

What makes HOAs legal entities — rather than informal neighborhood groups — is their incorporation under state (or in DC's case, District) law. In DC, most community associations are incorporated under the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act of 2010 (DC Code Title 29, Chapter 4). In Virginia, the applicable statute is the Virginia Nonstock Corporation Act (VA Code § 13.1-801 et seq.). In Maryland, it's the Maryland Nonstock Corporation Act (MD Code Corps & Assns § 5-101 et seq.).

This corporate structure matters for board members because it means the HOA can own property, sign contracts, sue and be sued, and take on debt — all in its own name. And it means the board is operating as the leadership of a legal corporate entity, with all the obligations that entails.

## The Board of Directors — Authority, Responsibility, and Accountability

Every HOA is governed by a board of directors. Board members are typically homeowners or unit owners within the community who are elected by their fellow members. In most communities, board service is voluntary and uncompensated.

This volunteer structure sometimes leads to an underestimation of what board membership actually requires. It's worth being direct: HOA board members in DC, Virginia, and Maryland carry fiduciary obligations to the association. These are legal duties, not aspirational standards.

**What the board is authorized to do** depends on the governing documents and the applicable statutes. Boards can adopt and enforce operating rules, approve budgets, authorize expenditures, hire and oversee contractors and management companies, collect assessments, and pursue enforcement actions against noncompliant owners. What the board cannot do is exceed the authority granted by the governing documents or contradict DC, Virginia, or Maryland law.

**What the board is personally responsible for** includes acting with care (making informed decisions, not rubber-stamping), acting loyally (putting the association's interest ahead of their own), and acting within the scope of their authority. A board member who authorizes an expenditure that benefits their own unit, ignores a conflict of interest, or makes major decisions without adequate information is not meeting these obligations.

**Protecting yourself.** The business judgment rule — recognized in DC, Virginia, and Maryland — provides meaningful protection to board members who can demonstrate they acted in good faith, with adequate information, and without personal financial interest in the outcome. Directors and Officers (D&O) liability insurance provides another layer of protection for covered claims. Both are important. If your association doesn't carry D&O coverage, addressing that gap should be a near-term board priority.

## HOA vs. Condo Association — A Distinction That Matters in DC Metro

In casual usage, "HOA" and "condo association" are often used interchangeably. In the DC metro area, they refer to legally distinct governance structures, and the distinction matters practically for boards.

**A homeowners association (HOA)** typically governs communities where owners hold title to both the interior of their unit and the land beneath it (or a defined plot of land). Common examples include single-family home subdivisions, townhouse communities, and some mixed-use developments. The HOA owns and maintains shared amenities and common areas. Owners are responsible for the exterior and interior of their own structures, depending on what the governing documents specify.

**A condominium association** governs communities where owners hold title to a defined interior space — the "air space" within their unit — and a proportional undivided interest in the common elements. The association owns and maintains the common elements, which typically include the building's structural components, exterior envelope, hallways, elevators, and shared mechanical systems. Individual unit owners own what's inside their walls but not the walls themselves.

**Why this distinction matters for DC boards:**

In DC, condominium associations are governed by the DC Condominium Act (DC Code § 42-1901 et seq.), which has specific requirements for how associations are formed, how boards operate, and what rights owners hold. Traditional HOAs in DC may be governed by DC's general nonprofit corporation statute and their own governing documents, without the same statutory overlay the Condominium Act provides.

In Virginia, planned community HOAs are governed by the Virginia Property Owners' Association Act (POAA, VA Code § 55.1-1800 et seq.), while condo associations fall under the Virginia Condominium Act (VA Code § 55.1-1900 et seq.). These are separate statutes with some important differences in board authority, assessment enforcement rights, and owner rights.

In Maryland, HOAs are governed primarily by the Maryland HOA Act (MD Code Real Property § 11B), while condo associations operate under the Maryland Condominium Act (§ 11-101 et seq.).

If you're not certain which statute primarily governs your association — particularly in Virginia, where both planned community and condominium law apply depending on community structure — review this with your management company or legal counsel.

## HOA Fees and Reserve Funds — What the Board Controls and What It Owes

One of the board's most consequential responsibilities is the annual budget — and with it, the assessment amounts that owners pay.

**How HOA fees are set.** The board prepares and approves an annual budget that covers the association's projected operating expenses (maintenance, insurance, management fees, landscaping, utilities for common areas, etc.) and its reserve contribution. The total budget divided by the assessment formula in the governing documents determines the per-unit fee for the year.

**What assessments cover.** Operating assessments fund day-to-day expenses. Reserve contributions fund the long-term replacement of major common components — roofs, elevators, paving, HVAC systems, pools — items that have finite useful lives and eventual replacement costs that can be substantial.

**The reserve funding obligation.** Boards have a fiduciary obligation to fund reserves adequately. An association that chronically underfunds reserves — keeping assessments artificially low — is deferring real costs onto future owners and future boards. When a major component fails and the reserve fund is insufficient, the result is typically a special assessment that can be significant and disruptive. Boards who make reserve funding decisions based on good-faith analysis of actual capital needs are meeting their duty. Boards who prioritize low assessments over long-term fiscal health are not.

**Special assessments.** When an unanticipated expense arises that the operating budget and reserves can't cover, the board may levy a special assessment — a one-time charge to all unit owners. The governing documents specify the process, including any owner vote requirements for assessments above a certain threshold. Special assessments should be documented with a board resolution and communicated clearly to owners.

## HOA Rules and Enforcement — Authority and Limits

HOAs have authority to adopt and enforce rules governing how the community operates. This authority comes from the governing documents and must be exercised consistently with applicable law.

**What HOA rules can govern.** Common areas of association rulemaking include: exterior property maintenance standards, architectural modification approvals, parking and vehicle restrictions, noise and nuisance standards, pet policies, rental and short-term rental restrictions, and common area use policies.

**What HOA rules cannot do.** Rules cannot violate federal or DC/Virginia/Maryland law. They cannot, for example, discriminate based on protected characteristics under the Fair Housing Act. They cannot prohibit display of the American flag in a way that violates the Freedom to Display the American Flag Act. They cannot restrict tenants with assistance animals from keeping those animals in ways that violate fair housing law. In Virginia, the POAA specifically limits which HOA rules are enforceable — and some rules that seem reasonable to a board may not be legally enforceable under Virginia law.

**Enforcement.** Rule enforcement requires consistency. Selective enforcement — applying rules to some owners but not others — creates both fairness problems and legal vulnerability. Most associations with sound enforcement programs have a written enforcement policy that specifies notice requirements, fine schedules, hearing rights, and escalation procedures. DC, Virginia, and Maryland each have requirements about notice and hearing before penalties are imposed; boards should ensure their enforcement process complies with the governing documents and applicable law.

## DC, Virginia, and Maryland — Three Distinct Frameworks

Board members overseeing communities that span multiple jurisdictions — or who move from a board in one jurisdiction to one in another — should understand that the legal framework is not the same across DC, Virginia, and Maryland.

**Washington DC.** DC condo associations operate under the DC Condominium Act, with additional governance obligations under the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act. DC's property laws, fair housing protections, and tenant rights statutes are generally more protective than Virginia or Maryland, which creates implications for associations with a mix of owner-occupied and investor-owned units.

**Virginia.** Virginia's framework is split between the POAA (planned communities) and the Virginia Condominium Act (condos). Virginia associations must comply with their specific statute, and the differences between the two statutes — in areas like assessment collection, lien priority, and owner rights — matter in practice. Northern Virginia communities in Arlington, Fairfax, Alexandria, and Tysons operate under Virginia state law, and county-level regulations can add additional requirements.

**Maryland.** Maryland's HOA Act and Condominium Act both apply in the DC suburb markets of Bethesda, Potomac, and surrounding Montgomery County and Prince George's County communities. Maryland has specific requirements about reserve disclosures to prospective buyers, and its resale disclosure requirements differ from Virginia and DC.

HOA communities aren't uniform. A 12-unit condominium building in Navy Yard, DC operates very differently from a 300-unit mixed-use community in Fairfax. The governance obligations — holding elections, managing finances, overseeing vendors, enforcing rules — scale with the community.

## When Boards Should Work with Professional Management

Professional management is not a substitute for a functional board — the board retains its legal authority and fiduciary responsibility regardless. But a qualified management company extends the board's capacity significantly.

Professional management companies typically handle:

- Day-to-day owner communication and service requests
- Vendor oversight, coordination, and bid management
- Financial management: budgeting, accounts payable, monthly financial reporting
- Assessment collection and delinquency follow-up
- Meeting preparation, agenda support, and minute-keeping
- Regulatory compliance monitoring (licensing, insurance requirements, jurisdiction-specific obligations)
- Coordination with association legal counsel

This frees board members to focus on governance — setting policy, making decisions, and overseeing performance — rather than operational tasks that consume time without building the board's capacity to lead.

The decision about whether to self-manage or hire a professional management company comes down to the board's time, expertise, and appetite for operational responsibility. Communities that self-manage often do so effectively when the board has members with relevant professional backgrounds and genuine bandwidth. When those conditions aren't consistently present, professional management is the more reliable path to stable governance.

## Frequently Asked Questions for Prospective and Current Board Members

**Do board members in DC HOAs have personal liability if the association makes a mistake?**  
Personal liability risk exists but is substantially reduced when board members act in good faith, with adequate information, and without conflicts of interest. The business judgment rule — recognized in DC, Virginia, and Maryland — protects boards who can demonstrate a genuine deliberation process. D&O liability insurance provides additional protection for covered claims. Associations that don't carry D&O coverage should address this gap.

**What's the difference between an HOA and a condo association in the DC metro market?**  
Structurally, HOAs typically govern communities where owners hold title to individual units and land, while condo associations govern communities where owners hold title to defined interior spaces and a proportional share of common elements. In practice, the governing statute differs: DC HOAs and condo associations have distinct legal frameworks, as do Virginia planned communities (POAA) and Virginia condo associations (Virginia Condominium Act). The right answer depends on how your community was legally formed.

**How is the board of a DC condo association elected?**  
Board elections in DC condo associations are governed by the association's bylaws and by the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act. Most condo bylaws specify the number of board seats, term lengths, eligibility requirements, and election procedures. Elections are typically held at the annual owner meeting, and the process must comply with both the bylaws and DC nonprofit corporate law. Improperly conducted elections can be challenged by members.

**Can our board raise assessments without a full owner vote?**  
In most community associations, the board has authority to set and adjust assessments within limits established by the governing documents. Many governing documents cap annual increases at a certain percentage (e.g., 10–15%) without requiring an owner vote, with a higher vote threshold required for increases beyond that cap or for special assessments above a specified amount. Review your specific governing documents — the answer varies by community.
