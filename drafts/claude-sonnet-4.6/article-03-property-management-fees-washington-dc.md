## The Standard Fee Structure: Four Components to Understand

Most DC metro property management companies charge fees across four categories. Understanding all four — not just the headline percentage — is essential to making an accurate cost comparison.

### 1. Monthly Management Fee

This is the ongoing fee for managing the property while it is occupied. In Washington DC, Northern Virginia, and Maryland, monthly management fees typically run between **7% and 10% of monthly collected rent**, depending on property type, location, number of units, and services included.

For a DC property renting at $2,400 per month (roughly a one-bedroom in Columbia Heights or Petworth), a 7% fee is $168 per month. At 10%, it is $240. For a $3,200 two-bedroom in Capitol Hill or Dupont Circle, those numbers are $224 and $320.

The critical question is whether the fee is charged on **collected rent** (what actually comes in) or **scheduled rent** (what the lease says should come in). A management company charging 8% on scheduled rent will charge you for their services even in months when the tenant does not pay — which is a meaningful difference if you ever face a delinquency.

### 2. Leasing Fee (Tenant Placement Fee)

When a new tenant is placed, most management companies charge a separate leasing fee. This covers the cost of marketing the property, showing it, screening applicants, preparing the lease, and conducting the move-in inspection.

In the DC market, leasing fees typically range from **50% to 100% of one month's rent**. For a property at $2,800 per month, this is $1,400 to $2,800 per tenancy. For a $4,500 luxury condo, it could be $2,250 to $4,500.

Some management companies advertise a lower monthly fee but a higher leasing fee. Others advertise a higher monthly fee and include the leasing service within it. Neither model is inherently better — it depends on your turnover rate, your property type, and how long your tenants typically stay. An owner in a building where tenants typically renew for three or four years will find the leasing fee less significant than an owner with frequent turnover.

### 3. Lease Renewal Fee

Not all management companies charge a renewal fee, but many do. Renewal fees are typically smaller — often $100 to $250 — and cover the cost of renegotiating lease terms, updating the agreement, conducting any required documentation, and coordinating with the tenant.

In DC, lease renewals are regulated by DC Code § 42-3505.53 for rent-controlled properties, which requires landlords to offer renewal unless there is a legal basis for non-renewal. Understanding renewal obligations — and ensuring your management company handles them correctly — is not just an administrative matter; it is a legal one.

### 4. Maintenance Coordination Fees

This is where fee comparisons get complicated. Some management companies mark up maintenance costs — charging homeowners a percentage (often 10–15%) above the contractor's actual invoice as a coordination fee. Others have in-house maintenance staff and charge time-based rates. Others include standard maintenance coordination in their monthly fee and only charge above a dollar threshold for major projects.

Ask any prospective management company:
- Do you add a markup to vendor invoices? If so, how much?
- Is there a threshold above which you require owner approval before authorizing a repair?
- Do you charge a project management fee for larger capital projects, and how is it calculated?

In a city like Washington DC where older housing stock — English basements, pre-war row houses, aging condo buildings in Capitol Hill and Georgetown — creates higher maintenance frequency, these fees add up. A management company charging an 8% monthly fee with a 15% maintenance markup might be meaningfully more expensive in practice than one charging 9% with no markup.

## What Good Property Management Actually Costs You Per Year

Let us build a realistic annual cost model for a DC property:

**Example property:** Two-bedroom condominium in Arlington, VA  
**Market rent:** $2,800/month  
**Tenant stability:** Lease renewed once during the year (no new placement)

| Fee | Estimate |
|-----|----------|
| Monthly management fee (8%) | $224/month × 12 = $2,688 |
| Lease renewal fee | $200 |
| Maintenance coordination (on $1,800 in repairs, no markup) | $0 |
| **Total annual management cost** | **~$2,888** |

As a percentage of gross rent collected ($33,600), that is about **8.6% of annual revenue** — which is a meaningful but not excessive cost of running a professionally managed rental asset.

Now consider the alternative: self-managing that same property. In Virginia, landlords are subject to VRLTA requirements (§ 55.1-1200 et seq.) — including disclosure obligations, maintenance response timelines, habitability standards, security deposit rules, and lease renewal notification requirements. A single misstep — an improperly handled security deposit, a maintenance failure that leads to a habitability complaint, a lease that does not comply with VRLTA — can generate legal exposure that dwarfs the annual management fee several times over.

## What Is (and Is Not) Included

This varies significantly by management company, which is why reading the management agreement carefully matters. Here is a typical breakdown:

**Usually included in the monthly management fee:**
- Tenant communication and relationship management
- Rent collection and disbursement to owner
- Monthly financial reporting (owner statement)
- Routine lease compliance monitoring
- Coordination of routine maintenance requests (within a defined dollar threshold)
- Access to an owner portal for real-time visibility

**Usually charged separately:**
- Tenant placement / leasing fee
- Lease renewal fee
- Maintenance markups (varies by company)
- Inspection fees (some companies charge $75–$150 per annual or move-in/move-out inspection)
- Eviction coordination fees (typically $200–$500 above whatever legal costs apply)
- Major capital project oversight

**Not included anywhere:**
- Actual maintenance and repair costs (these are paid directly from owner funds)
- Legal fees if an eviction or dispute requires an attorney
- DC DCRA inspection fees or licensing costs
- Lead paint inspection or clearance costs if required

## Why DC's Regulatory Environment Affects the True Value of Professional Management

DC has a legal compliance environment that adds meaningful ongoing cost and risk for self-managing owners. Any property management fee analysis should factor in the regulatory work a good management company is doing on your behalf:

**Business license (BBL) maintenance:** DC Code § 47-2851.03 requires a current rental property business license from DLCP. A management company maintains this on your behalf. Operating without it exposes you to fines up to $2,000.

**Rent control compliance:** Properties with five or more units built before 1976 are subject to DC rent stabilization under § 42-3501. This requires DHCD registration, CPI-W-compliant rent increases, and annual reporting. Errors in rent control compliance create significant liability.

**TOPA notifications:** When a DC rental property is offered for sale, TOPA (§ 42-3404) requires specific tenant notifications and waiting periods. A management company familiar with TOPA can prevent the kind of procedural errors that can complicate or block a sale.

**DC Tenant Bill of Rights:** Every new DC tenant must receive a signed copy of the Tenant Bill of Rights (§ 42-3502.22). This is not paperwork — it is a legal requirement.

**Lead paint disclosure:** DC requires additional lead paint disclosure beyond federal requirements. A management company tracking this obligation prevents one of the more common and costly compliance failures for DC owners.

The aggregate compliance value of having these obligations managed correctly — especially for out-of-state or inattentive owners — easily justifies the management fee for most DC properties.

## Comparing Proposals: What to Ask Before Signing

When you receive management proposals, bring these specific questions:

1. Is the monthly fee on collected or scheduled rent?
2. What is the leasing fee structure, and does it apply to renewals as well as new tenants?
3. Is there a maintenance markup? What is the percentage and where is it disclosed in the agreement?
4. Are there inspection fees, and if so, what triggers them?
5. What are the termination provisions? How much notice is required and is there a financial penalty?
6. What does monthly owner reporting include, and can I see a sample statement?
7. Who manages the property on a day-to-day basis, and how is the workload structured?

A management company that gives clear, specific answers to these questions without hesitation is operating transparently. One that deflects, pivots to marketing language, or tells you the contract "explains everything" without explaining it in the conversation deserves more scrutiny.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the typical property management fee in Washington DC?**  
Most DC metro property management companies charge between 7% and 10% of monthly collected rent as a management fee. Leasing fees for new tenant placement are typically 50–100% of one month's rent, charged separately. Companies with lower headline management fees sometimes have higher leasing fees or maintenance markups — always evaluate total annual cost rather than just the monthly percentage.

**Do property managers in DC charge a leasing fee every time a tenant renews?**  
Most companies charge a smaller renewal fee ($100–$250 is common) rather than a full leasing fee for renewals, since renewal requires less work than a new placement. Some companies waive the renewal fee entirely. This should be clearly specified in the management agreement.

**Is a 10% property management fee too high in DC?**  
Not necessarily. A 10% fee that includes comprehensive services, transparent maintenance coordination, no markups, and deep DC compliance expertise may represent better value than a 7% fee with multiple add-ons and limited local legal knowledge. Evaluate total annual cost and what is included — not just the percentage.

**Who pays for repairs — the owner or the management company?**  
Repairs and maintenance costs are always paid by the property owner from owner funds. The management company coordinates the work, but the cost is not their expense. What varies is whether the management company adds a coordination markup on top of the vendor's invoice.

**What happens if my tenant doesn't pay — am I still charged a management fee?**  
This depends on whether the management company charges on collected rent or scheduled rent. Companies that charge on collected rent do not charge a fee for months with no payment. Companies that charge on scheduled rent do. This distinction matters and should be confirmed before signing.

**What should I look for in a property management agreement in DC?**  
Look for clarity on fee structure, termination provisions, maintenance authorization limits, reporting obligations, and who holds and manages the security deposit. For DC specifically, confirm the management company understands and handles BBL compliance, TOPA obligations, rent control registration, and the Tenant Bill of Rights.
