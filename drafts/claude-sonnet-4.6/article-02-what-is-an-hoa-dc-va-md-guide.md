A homeowners association — commonly called an HOA — is a governing body created to manage and maintain the shared elements of a residential community. If you own a home, condo, or townhouse in a planned community, condominium building, or gated subdivision, there is a good chance an HOA or condo association governs it.

For most residents, an HOA is just part of the background — monthly dues, community rules, and occasional mailings. For board members, it is something quite different. Serving on an HOA board means accepting fiduciary responsibilities, navigating real estate law, managing budgets, overseeing vendors, and making decisions that affect dozens or hundreds of homeowners and their property values.

This guide explains what an HOA is, how it works, and what board members in Washington DC, Northern Virginia, and Maryland specifically need to understand — because the legal framework governing community associations varies meaningfully across these jurisdictions.

---

## What an HOA Actually Is

An HOA is a nonprofit corporate entity — typically incorporated as a nonstock corporation — created when a real estate developer records a declaration of covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs) on a community's land. That declaration creates a mandatory governing structure that binds every property owner in the community when they purchase.

The HOA has three primary functions:

1. **Governance:** Setting and enforcing community rules, policies, and standards through governing documents (CC&Rs, bylaws, rules and regulations)
2. **Financial management:** Collecting assessments (dues) from homeowners and using that money to maintain common areas, fund reserves, and pay for shared services
3. **Property maintenance:** Managing and preserving shared elements — entrances, amenities, landscaping, building exteriors, elevators, roofs (in condos), and anything else the governing documents define as a common element

The distinction between an HOA and a condo association matters. In a typical HOA (usually single-family homes or townhouses), homeowners own their lots and exterior structures, and the HOA manages the shared infrastructure. In a condo association, homeowners own their individual units but the association owns and manages everything outside those units — the building envelope, common hallways, roofs, mechanical systems, and shared spaces. This distinction affects legal authority, insurance obligations, and how maintenance responsibilities are allocated.

## The Governing Documents That Define Authority

Every HOA operates through a hierarchy of governing documents:

- **Declaration of CC&Rs:** The foundational document recorded in public land records. It defines the community structure, the association's authority, common elements, and the covenants all owners are bound by.
- **Bylaws:** Govern the internal operation of the association — how meetings are conducted, how board members are elected, term lengths, quorum requirements, and officer responsibilities.
- **Rules and Regulations:** Created by the board (within limits set by the CC&Rs) to govern day-to-day community standards — parking, pets, noise, architectural changes, move-in procedures.
- **Budget and reserve fund policies:** Financial documents that govern how assessments are set, how reserves are funded, and how capital expenditures are approved.

When a board decision is challenged or a dispute arises, the first question is always: what do the governing documents say? Rules that contradict state law are unenforceable. Rules that the board created without the authority granted by the CC&Rs may also be unenforceable. Board members who do not read and understand their governing documents are governing by intuition rather than authority.

## HOA Law by Jurisdiction: What DC, Virginia, and Maryland Board Members Need to Know

This is where generic HOA guides fall short. The legal framework for community associations is set at the state or local level — and the DC metro area spans three distinct jurisdictions, each with its own statutes.

### Washington DC

Condominium associations in DC operate under the **DC Condominium Act, DC Code § 42-1901 et seq.** This statute governs condo formation, unit owner rights, board authority, assessment collection, and common element maintenance. DC does not have a separate HOA statute comparable to Virginia's — most planned communities in DC are structured as condominiums rather than homeowners associations.

Key DC obligations board members should understand:
- Associations must maintain a reserve fund for capital repairs and replacements (§ 42-1903.13)
- Board meetings must be open to unit owners (§ 42-1903.09), with limited executive session exceptions
- Assessment liens and collection procedures are governed by § 42-1903.13, which includes DC's super-lien provision — HOA assessments up to six months have priority over a first mortgage in certain circumstances
- The DC Consumer Protection Procedures Act can apply to HOA communications and sales of units

### Virginia

Virginia community associations are governed primarily by two statutes:
- **Property Owners' Association Act (POAA), VA Code § 55.1-1800 et seq.:** Applies to planned unit developments and HOAs with single-family or townhouse properties
- **Virginia Condominium Act, VA Code § 55.1-1900 et seq.:** Applies to condominium associations

Both statutes set baseline requirements that associations cannot override through their governing documents. Important requirements include:
- Annual budget preparation and distribution to owners (POAA § 55.1-1825 for HOAs; Condo Act § 55.1-1964 for condos)
- Mandatory reserve study or reserve plan for associations meeting size thresholds
- Open meeting requirements with defined executive session categories (POAA § 55.1-1816)
- Owner access rights to association records (POAA § 55.1-1815)
- Common Interest Community (CIC) Ombudsman through the Office of the Common Interest Community Ombudsman (CICB), which provides dispute resolution and regulatory oversight

Virginia also licenses community association managers through DPOR. If your association in Arlington, Fairfax, or Alexandria hires a professional management company, that company must hold appropriate DPOR credentials (CAPLM designation).

### Maryland

Maryland HOAs and condo associations are governed by:
- **Maryland HOA Act, Real Property § 11B:** Applies to homeowners associations
- **Maryland Condominium Act, Real Property § 11-101 et seq.:** Applies to condo associations

Maryland requires associations to provide budget information to homeowners annually (§ 11B-112) and establishes owner rights to attend meetings and access records (§ 11B-111). Maryland's reserve fund requirements have been strengthened in recent years — associations must conduct or update reserve studies and disclose reserve fund status to prospective purchasers.

Montgomery County and Prince George's County — where Bethesda, Potomac, and suburban DC communities are concentrated — may have additional local requirements that overlay state law.

## What HOA Dues Actually Pay For

HOA dues (assessments) fund two buckets: operating expenses and reserves.

**Operating expenses** are the recurring costs of running the community: landscaping, common area cleaning, exterior lighting, insurance, management fees, utility costs for shared areas, administrative expenses, and routine maintenance.

**Reserves** are funds set aside for major capital repairs and replacements that will be needed in the future — roof replacements, elevator overhauls, pool resurfacing, parking lot repaving, HVAC replacements for common areas. Reserve funding is not optional. An under-funded reserve fund is one of the most serious problems an HOA can have — it leads either to large special assessments on homeowners or deferred maintenance that accelerates the deterioration of the community's shared assets.

In the DC metro area, HOA dues vary significantly by community type, size, amenities, and age. A smaller townhome community in Fairfax County with minimal amenities might run $200–$350 per month. A full-service high-rise condo building in DC or Bethesda with a doorperson, pool, gym, and extensive building systems might run $700–$1,500 per month or more. Dues in older buildings in Arlington, Capitol Hill, and Dupont Circle often reflect the higher capital maintenance demands of aging building systems.

## The Board's Fiduciary Responsibilities

Serving on an HOA board is a legal responsibility, not just a volunteer role. Board members owe the association and its homeowners a duty of care and a duty of loyalty — the two core fiduciary obligations that govern nonprofit corporate boards.

**Duty of care** means making informed, reasonably prudent decisions. This includes reading governing documents before making decisions, understanding the association's finances before approving a budget, and seeking professional advice (legal, engineering, financial) for significant matters rather than guessing.

**Duty of loyalty** means acting in the best interests of the community rather than personal interests. A board member who steers a landscaping contract to their own business without disclosure is in violation of this duty. So is a board member who selectively enforces rules against neighbors they dislike.

In Virginia, the business judgment rule provides some protection for board decisions made in good faith — but it requires that the decision was actually made in good faith and with reasonable information. In DC, the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act provides similar framework for board liability.

Directors and Officers (D&O) insurance is an important protection for board members. Most governing documents require the association to carry it. If yours does not, the board should seriously discuss adding it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between an HOA and a condo association?**  
Both are community associations that govern shared property. In an HOA (typically single-family homes or townhomes), each owner holds title to their lot and exterior structure. In a condo association, unit owners own only their interior space — everything outside the unit is common area owned and maintained by the association. The legal frameworks differ: in Virginia, HOAs fall under the POAA (§ 55.1-1800) and condos under the Condo Act (§ 55.1-1900). In DC, condominiums are governed by the DC Condominium Act (§ 42-1901). In Maryland, the HOA Act (§ 11B) and Condo Act (§ 11-101) govern separately.

**Can an HOA foreclose on a home in DC, Virginia, or Maryland?**  
Yes, in all three jurisdictions, HOAs and condo associations have lien rights for unpaid assessments and can pursue foreclosure under certain conditions. In DC, the condo association's super-lien provision (§ 42-1903.13(h)) gives it priority over a first mortgage for up to six months of assessments. In Virginia, POAA § 55.1-1833 establishes lien rights and foreclosure authority. In Maryland, Real Property § 11B-117 provides comparable authority. Boards should work with legal counsel on assessment collection and lien enforcement procedures.

**Are HOA rules enforceable in Virginia?**  
Most are, but not all. Virginia law identifies categories of HOA rules that are unenforceable — including rules that violate state law, federal law (such as the FCC's OTARD rule on satellite dishes), or fair housing requirements. Virginia's Common Interest Community Ombudsman can receive complaints about governing document enforcement. Boards should have an attorney review rules before adopting and enforcing them to avoid exposure.

**What is a reserve study and does our HOA need one?**  
A reserve study is a professional assessment of the association's physical assets and their projected replacement costs over time. It informs how much the association should be setting aside in reserves each year. Virginia's POAA requires reserve studies for qualifying associations (§ 55.1-1825). DC's Condo Act addresses reserve fund adequacy requirements (§ 42-1903.13). Maryland's reserve study requirements have been strengthened through recent legislative updates. Associations without a current reserve study are operating with significant financial blind spots.

**How does professional HOA management work?**  
A professional HOA management company takes over the day-to-day operations of the community on behalf of the board. This typically includes financial management (collecting dues, paying vendors, preparing financial reports, managing reserves), vendor oversight, maintenance coordination, meeting support, compliance monitoring, and owner communication.
