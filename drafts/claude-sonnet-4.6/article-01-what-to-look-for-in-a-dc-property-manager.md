The search for a DC property manager should feel consequential. Not because the market is full of bad operators, but because Washington DC's rental regulatory environment is complex enough that the wrong management company can leave you exposed to legal liability, missed compliance obligations, and financial loss that goes well beyond a few months of subpar service.

This isn't an abstract concern. DC is one of the most heavily regulated rental markets in the country. Before a single tenant moves in, your property needs a valid rental business license. If your building was constructed before 1975 and isn't exempt, rent control likely applies — affecting what you charge, when you can raise rent, and what you must file annually. Before you sell, a law called TOPA gives your tenants the legal right to purchase ahead of any third-party buyer. When you screen applicants, the Fair Criminal Record Screening Act limits how and when you can consider criminal history. These obligations don't disappear because you hired a property manager — they remain your responsibility, and the company you hire determines how well you meet them.

So the standard checklist — check reviews, compare fees, look for full service — is necessary but not sufficient. Here's what the evaluation actually needs to include.

---

## 1. Verify Licensing Before Anything Else

This is the non-negotiable first step, and it's DC-specific.

Any company collecting rent or placing tenants on behalf of property owners in DC must hold an active DC real estate broker's license. Managing property in DC without one is operating illegally, and any management agreements or tenant placements they execute could be unenforceable or create liability for you.

Beyond the broker license, every rental property in Washington DC must have a valid Basic Business License (BBL) — the city's rental registration, issued by the DC Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection (DLCP). Under DC Code § 47-2851.03, renting property without a valid BBL is a violation. A qualified property manager should take full ownership of obtaining and renewing your BBL, know whether your property requires a housing inspection before licensure, and understand which license category applies to your property type.

To verify a company's real estate broker license, use the DC Real Estate Commission's license lookup through DLCP. It takes two minutes and should be the first thing you confirm.

## 2. DC Regulatory Fluency — Where Good Managers Are Separated from Average Ones

Licensing verifies that a company can legally operate. Regulatory fluency determines whether they can actually protect you. Ask any candidate how they handle each of the following:

**Rent stabilization.** Under DC Code § 42-3501 et seq., most buildings constructed before 1975 are subject to rent stabilization unless they qualify for a specific exemption. This affects initial pricing when a unit turns over, annual rent increase limits, required annual registration filings with the DC Rental Accommodations Division, and how you document any capital improvement petitions. A property manager who doesn't flag this early — or who doesn't know your building's rent stabilization status — is already behind.

**TOPA — Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act.** Before you sell a rental property in DC, DC Code § 42-3404 requires that you give tenants a formal opportunity to purchase the property. The process is multi-step, has specific timeline requirements, and missing a step can complicate or delay a sale. Your property manager probably isn't handling the transaction itself, but they should understand TOPA well enough to prepare you for what the process involves and to ensure tenant relations are properly maintained throughout.

**Tenant Bill of Rights.** DC law requires landlords to provide tenants with a specific notice — the DC Tenant Bill of Rights — at move-in. This document informs tenants of their rights under DC law. It is mandatory, and failure to provide it is a compliance violation. A good property manager provides this systematically, not as an afterthought.

**Fair Criminal Record Screening Act.** Under DC Code § 2-1402.68, landlords cannot ask about criminal history on an initial rental application. Inquiries about criminal background can only occur after a conditional offer of housing has been made — and even then, the law restricts how that information can be used. Property managers who rely on outdated blanket screening practices are putting you at risk of a fair housing complaint.

**Lead paint.** DC requires specific lead paint disclosures for properties built before 1978. When households include children under six or pregnant women, the requirements intensify to include inspections and clearance. Ask directly how the company handles this — both disclosure at move-in and monitoring during the tenancy.

## 3. Fee Structure — Read the Contract, Not Just the Headline Rate

The monthly management fee percentage gets a lot of attention, but it's rarely the source of the most important fee issues. What matters more is how fees are structured and what obligations they create.

**Collected vs. scheduled rent.** Some property management companies charge their monthly fee based on the amount of rent your tenant *owes* rather than the rent they *paid*. This means you pay the management fee even during vacancies or when a tenant is delinquent. Fee structures based on collected rent align the management company's incentives with yours.

**Leasing fees.** A leasing fee — typically one month's rent — covers the work of finding and placing a new tenant. Ask exactly what that includes: marketing, photography, showings, application processing, screening, lease execution, and move-in inspection. Some companies charge a leasing fee and then bill separately for items that should be included.

**Lease renewal fees.** Renewal fees are standard. What matters is the amount and what the company actually does for that fee — a new lease agreement, renewal pricing analysis, and tenant communication should all be included.

**Maintenance markups.** Many property management companies mark up vendor invoices — sometimes by 10%, sometimes significantly more. Ask directly whether they mark up maintenance costs, by how much, and whether you'll see the original vendor invoice. A company that provides full vendor invoice transparency and charges no markup is structuring their maintenance program in your interest.

**Exit terms.** What does it take to terminate the management agreement? Are there fees? Are there notice periods that leave you in limbo? Review the full agreement before signing, not just the fee schedule.

## 4. Leasing Quality — Placement Matters More Than Speed

Vacancy is costly, but placing the wrong tenant costs more. When evaluating a company's leasing approach, ask:

- How do they price a listing, and do they benchmark against current DC market data?
- What marketing platforms do they use, and how long do comparable properties typically sit?
- How does their screening process comply with DC's Fair Criminal Record Screening Act?
- What does their lease template look like — does it include DC-required notices, rent stabilization disclosures where applicable, and DC-specific addenda?
- How do they handle lead paint disclosures at move-in?

A lease template not designed for DC — or one that hasn't been updated to reflect current DC law — is a recurring liability every time a new tenant signs.

---

## 5. Maintenance — Transparency and Accountability

Maintenance is where the quality of a property management company often becomes most visible. Ask:

- Do they use in-house maintenance staff, outside vendors, or a combination?
- What's their emergency maintenance response protocol?
- Do they mark up vendor invoices? If so, by how much, and is the original invoice available to you?
- Do they require quotes for maintenance work above a certain dollar amount?
- How are maintenance requests tracked, and how are you notified?

For DC properties, maintenance also intersects with regulatory compliance: habitability standards under DC law require that landlords maintain properties in good repair, and certain conditions — heat below minimum temperatures, water issues, pest infestations — require specific response timelines. Ask whether the company trains their team on these obligations.

---

## 6. Communication and Owner Reporting

At minimum, expect an owner portal with real-time access to financial statements, maintenance records, and tenant information. Monthly financial reports should be legible, not just data exports. You should receive 1099s and supporting documentation for tax purposes annually without having to chase them.

More important than the tools: ask how they communicate when something goes wrong. When a tenant doesn't pay, when a maintenance issue escalates, when a compliance question comes up — what does that process look like? Who is your point of contact, and what happens if that person changes?

---

## 7. References — Go Beyond the Testimonials

Ask for direct references from current clients with similar property types in similar DC neighborhoods. Generic testimonials are curated. A direct conversation with another DC owner — one who has dealt with maintenance issues, lease renewals, tenant disputes, or a difficult compliance situation — tells you far more.

Specific questions to ask references:
- How did the company handle a difficult tenant situation?
- Were there any charges that surprised you?
- Did they keep you informed when something went wrong?
- Would you use them again, and why?

## Red Flags

- **Cannot provide a verifiable DC real estate broker license number**
- **Vague about fee structure or reluctant to provide a written fee schedule**
- **No mention of rent stabilization, TOPA, or the Tenant Bill of Rights during the evaluation conversation**
- **Lease templates that lack DC-required disclosures or haven't been updated recently**
- **Opaque maintenance billing — can't or won't show you original vendor invoices**
- **Long lock-in contracts with no performance exit clause**
- **No clear point of contact for your account**

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I verify a DC property management company's real estate broker license?**  
You can search active DC real estate broker licenses through the DC Department of Licensing and Consumer Protection's license verification tool at mylicense.eregulations.dc.gov. All active licenses are publicly listed. Verify before signing any management agreement.

**Does my rental property need a DC Business License (BBL) if I hire a property manager?**  
Yes. The BBL is a property-level license required for every residential rental property in DC under DC Code § 47-2851.03. Your property manager should manage the application and renewal on your behalf, but the obligation is tied to your property — not their company.

**What is TOPA and does it affect me if I'm not planning to sell?**  
TOPA — the Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act — applies when you decide to sell. It requires you to formally notify tenants and provide them the opportunity to purchase before you can sell to a third party. Even if you're not planning to sell now, understanding how your management company would handle a TOPA situation matters. Ask whether they've managed it before.

**What's a typical management fee range for DC residential property management?**  
Fees for single properties in the DC market generally fall between 7% and 10% of collected rent, with variation based on property type, location, and included services. Be cautious of fees at the very low end of the market without a clear explanation of what's included and what costs extra.

**If my property manager makes a compliance mistake, am I legally responsible?**  
Yes. As the property owner, you are ultimately the responsible party under DC law. A well-structured management agreement should include language about the company's liability for errors made in their management duties. Review the contract carefully, and make sure any compliance responsibilities are explicitly assigned and confirmed in writing.
