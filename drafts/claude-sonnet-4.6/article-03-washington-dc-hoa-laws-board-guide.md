Serving on an HOA or condo association board in Washington DC carries legal weight that many board members underestimate when they first take the seat. DC's governance framework for community associations is built on several distinct statutes, a layered hierarchy of governing documents, and a set of fiduciary obligations that apply to every board member personally — not just the association as an entity.

Understanding the legal landscape isn't about becoming an attorney. It's about knowing what the law requires of your board, where your authority comes from, and where the limits of that authority lie. Boards that operate with this clarity manage their communities more effectively and take on less personal liability. Boards that don't often find themselves managing consequences they could have avoided.

Here's a working guide to DC HOA and condo law for people who are actually running these communities.

---

## The DC Condominium Act — The Foundation for Most DC Communities

The DC Condominium Act, found at DC Code Title 42, Chapter 19 (§ 42-1901 et seq.), is the primary statute governing condominium associations in the District. If you're on the board of a DC condo association, this is the law that defines the outer boundaries of your authority and the rights of your unit owners.

The Act covers:

**Creation and structure.** Condos in DC must be created through a declaration filed with the DC Recorder of Deeds. The declaration establishes the boundaries between private units and common elements, defines what the association owns and maintains, and sets the baseline governance framework.

**Association powers.** Under § 42-1903.08, condo associations in DC have the authority to collect assessments and dues, maintain and repair common elements, hire vendors and contractors, enter into contracts, adopt and amend operating rules, and pursue or defend legal action on behalf of the association. These powers exist because the statute grants them — the association cannot act beyond what the Act and its governing documents authorize.

**Unit owner rights.** The Act also establishes rights on the other side: owners have the right to inspect association records (§ 42-1903.13), to use common elements on equal terms, and to participate in governance. Board decisions that violate these owner rights — for example, restricting access to financial records without cause — create legal exposure.

**Assessment liens.** DC's Condominium Act gives associations the right to place a lien on a unit for unpaid assessments. The lien process is governed by § 42-1903.13 and requires specific notice and procedure. Understanding lien rights matters practically: it's one of the primary tools associations have to enforce payment obligations.

## The Horizontal Property Act — Applies to Older DC Condominiums

DC's Horizontal Property Act (DC Code Title 42, Chapter 20) predates the Condominium Act and continues to govern DC condominiums that were legally created under the older statute — generally condominiums formed before the DC Condominium Act was enacted in 1976.

If your association was formed under the Horizontal Property Act, the governing framework is similar in concept but older and less detailed in its requirements. Many associations originally formed under the Horizontal Property Act have since adopted governing documents that align with the standards of the newer Condominium Act, but the foundational legal framework remains the older statute.

Boards of older DC condo communities should confirm which law governs their association and ensure their current governing documents are consistent with that framework. Mismatches between an association's governing documents and the statute that applies to it can create governance ambiguity that professional management or legal counsel should help resolve.

## The DC Nonprofit Corporation Act — Governing How Your Board Functions

Most DC HOAs and condo associations are organized as nonprofit corporations. This subjects them to the DC Nonprofit Corporation Act of 2010 (DC Code Title 29, Chapter 4), which governs how the corporate entity functions — independent of the property-specific rules in the Condominium Act.

This statute is where boards often have the most practical gaps in their knowledge, because it covers:

**Board elections and structure.** The Act sets requirements for how board members are elected, how vacancies are filled, and what constitutes a valid board action. If your bylaws are silent on a particular governance question, the Nonprofit Corporation Act typically fills the gap.

**Meeting requirements.** Board meetings must meet notice and quorum requirements. The Act provides default rules for these requirements when the governing documents don't specify them. Boards that fail to meet quorum, provide inadequate notice, or conduct votes through impermissible informal means may find their actions subject to challenge.

**Recordkeeping obligations.** DC nonprofit corporations are required to maintain specific records, including minutes of board and membership meetings, financial records, and the current roster of members. These are not merely best practices — they're legal requirements.

**Director duties.** The Nonprofit Corporation Act articulates the baseline standard of conduct for board directors, which overlaps with the general fiduciary obligations discussed below.

## Board Member Fiduciary Duties — What You Are Personally Responsible For

This is the area most board members understand least when they first join a board. Serving as a board member in DC creates personal legal duties. These duties don't disappear because you're a volunteer or because the association is a nonprofit.

**Duty of care.** Board members must make decisions with the care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in a similar situation. This means actually reviewing financial statements before approving them, getting appropriate bids for major repair projects, and not rubber-stamping decisions without understanding them. A board member who votes to approve a major vendor contract without reading it or asking questions is not meeting their duty of care.

**Duty of loyalty.** Board members must act in the best interest of the association, not their own personal interest or the interest of a subset of owners. This most commonly becomes an issue when a board member has a financial interest in a vendor the association is considering, when decisions benefit the board member's own unit at community expense, or when a board member uses their position to pursue a personal dispute with a neighbor. DC and the Nonprofit Corporation Act require disclosure of conflicts of interest, and board members with conflicts should recuse themselves from related decisions.

**Business judgment rule.** When board members act in good faith, with adequate information, and without self-interest, the business judgment rule provides them significant protection from personal liability for decisions that turn out badly. This protection depends on the board demonstrating a real process — deliberation, information-gathering, good-faith reasoning — not just arriving at a defensible outcome.

**D&O Insurance.** Most DC community associations carry Directors and Officers (D&O) liability insurance, which provides coverage for board members facing claims related to their governance decisions. If your association doesn't currently carry D&O coverage, this is a material risk gap that the board should address. A professional management company can help you assess appropriate coverage levels.

## The Governing Document Hierarchy

DC law doesn't govern every aspect of your association's operations directly. The statutes set a framework, and your association's governing documents fill in the details. The hierarchy of authority works like this:

1. **DC Statutes** (Condominium Act, Nonprofit Corporation Act) — the top of the hierarchy; governing documents cannot conflict with these.
2. **Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs)** — establishes the fundamental structure of the community, owner obligations, and the association's authority. Changes typically require a supermajority vote of the owners.
3. **Bylaws** — govern how the board operates: elections, meetings, officer duties, quorum requirements, voting procedures.
4. **Operating Rules and Resolutions** — day-to-day rules adopted by the board (parking, noise, pet policies, common area use). These are the easiest to amend — typically by board vote — but cannot conflict with the documents above them.

When a conflict exists between levels of this hierarchy, the higher document controls. A board cannot adopt an operating rule that contradicts the CC&Rs, and the CC&Rs cannot authorize something the DC Condominium Act prohibits.

Understanding this hierarchy matters practically: when a board member argues that "the rules say we can do this," the first question is always which rules, and whether those rules are themselves valid under the documents above them.

## Assessment Collection and Enforcement

Assessments are the financial lifeblood of the association. When owners don't pay, the association can't meet its obligations — maintenance contracts, insurance, reserves, management fees. DC law gives associations real enforcement tools, and boards should understand them.

Under the DC Condominium Act (§ 42-1903.13), associations have a lien right for unpaid assessments. The lien attaches to the unit and has priority over most other encumbrances except first mortgages. To enforce the lien, the association must follow specific notice and recording procedures. For significant delinquencies, associations can ultimately pursue foreclosure on the lien — though this is a remedy used carefully and typically after other collection efforts have failed.

Practically speaking, the most effective collection programs are also the most consistent ones. Clear written policies for when late notices are sent, when accounts go to counsel, and what payment plan options exist — applied uniformly across all units — are both legally and operationally sound. Boards that make ad hoc collection exceptions create inequity and legal risk.

## Reserve Fund Obligations

DC does not currently mandate reserve studies by statute in the way some other states do, but the absence of a statutory mandate doesn't mean boards can neglect reserves without consequence.

The DC Condominium Act's requirement that boards maintain common elements in good repair creates an implied obligation to plan for major component replacements. Associations that chronically underfund reserves — and then face a major repair they can't afford — typically end up levying large special assessments that create owner hardship and board member accountability. Boards who can demonstrate they made good-faith, informed decisions about reserve funding (based on engineering assessments or reserve studies) are in a far better legal and practical position than boards who simply adopted the lowest budget the owners would approve.

For associations managing communities with significant common elements — elevators, roofing systems, HVAC, parking structures, pools — periodic reserve studies conducted by a qualified professional are a governance best practice regardless of what DC law requires. A professional management company can connect your board with qualified reserve study professionals and help you incorporate reserve funding into your annual budget process.

## Seller Disclosures and the Association's Role

DC's Residential Real Property Seller Disclosure Act (DC Code Title 42, Chapter 13) requires property sellers to disclose known material defects before the sale. For condo and HOA properties, this often includes disclosures about monthly fees, pending special assessments, and any pending litigation involving the association.

Associations have a role here: when unit owners sell, they typically need a resale certificate or estoppel certificate from the association confirming the unit's assessment payment status, any outstanding violations, and any pending assessments. Many DC associations charge a fee for preparing this document. Boards should have a clear process for responding to these requests promptly — delays can affect sale timelines and create friction with owners.

## When to Involve Professional Management or Legal Counsel

DC HOA and condo law is detailed enough that most boards benefit from professional support — either from a qualified property management company or from an HOA attorney (or both). Specific situations where professional involvement is particularly important:

- **Governing document amendments.** Changes to CC&Rs or bylaws require careful legal review to ensure they're consistent with DC law and won't create unintended consequences.
- **Assessment enforcement and lien actions.** These involve formal legal procedures and should be handled with counsel's guidance.
- **Board elections and governance disputes.** DC nonprofit law and governing documents both apply; an improperly conducted election can be challenged.
- **Major contracts and vendor agreements.** Review by a professional management company or attorney protects the board from unfavorable terms.
- **Insurance claims and litigation.** Any actual or threatened legal action against or by the association warrants counsel's involvement.

## Frequently Asked Questions for DC Board Members

**Does DC law require our condo association to conduct a reserve study?**  
DC does not currently mandate reserve studies by statute. However, the association's obligation to maintain common elements and exercise proper fiduciary care creates a strong practical case for them. Boards who make reserve funding decisions based on current information — from a reserve study, an engineering assessment, or a capital needs analysis — are better positioned legally than those who don't. Many professional management companies can help you identify reserve study professionals and integrate the findings into your budgeting process.

**Can our board adopt new operating rules without a vote of all unit owners?**  
Generally, yes — operating rules can be adopted or amended by board vote without requiring a full owner vote, as long as the rules don't conflict with the CC&Rs or bylaws. However, the process must comply with whatever notice and meeting requirements your governing documents specify. Some major rule changes — particularly those affecting owner rights to use common elements — may require broader notice or owner input. When in doubt, review the proposed rule against the governing documents.

**What records are owners entitled to inspect in a DC condo association?**  
Under DC Code § 42-1903.13, unit owners have the right to inspect certain association records, including financial records and meeting minutes. Your governing documents may specify additional record rights. Boards should have a clear process for responding to record inspection requests. Unreasonable denial of inspection rights is a violation of the Condominium Act.

**How do we handle a unit owner who consistently refuses to pay assessments?**  
DC law gives associations lien rights for unpaid assessments under § 42-1903.13. The practical collection process typically moves through: written late notices, assessment of late fees (as authorized by governing documents), referral to association counsel for a formal demand, recording a lien, and ultimately lien foreclosure for significant delinquencies. Many associations adopt written collection policies that describe each step and apply them consistently across all units. Consistent application reduces the risk of claims that the board is treating owners unequally.

**As a DC board member, am I personally liable if the association makes a bad decision?**  
Personal liability risk exists but is significantly mitigated when board members: act in good faith with adequate information, disclose and recuse themselves from conflicts of interest, follow the association's governing documents and DC law, and ensure the association carries appropriate D&O insurance. Board members who can demonstrate a genuine deliberation process — rather than arbitrary or self-interested decision-making — have strong protection under the business judgment rule. D&O insurance provides an additional layer of protection for covered claims.
