## What "Third-Party Property Management" Actually Means

Third-party property management refers to a contractual arrangement in which an independent management company — not the property owner, and not an employee of the owner — is hired to operate a multifamily property on the owner's behalf.

The term "third-party" distinguishes this model from two alternatives: self-management (where the owner manages directly, either personally or through employees they hire) and fee management structures where a related entity manages for a sister company or affiliated investor group.

In a third-party management arrangement:

- The management company acts as the owner's agent
- The management company typically handles all day-to-day operations: leasing, rent collection, maintenance coordination, resident communication, vendor oversight, and compliance monitoring
- The owner retains decision-making authority over major capital decisions, budget approval, and strategic direction
- The management company is compensated by a management fee — typically a percentage of collected revenue — rather than profiting from the property directly

The arrangement is formalized in a management agreement that defines scope, fees, reporting requirements, owner and manager authority, termination rights, and liability provisions.

## Self-Management vs. Third-Party Management vs. In-House Management

Multifamily owners in DC metro generally have three options for how they staff the management function:

**Self-management** means the owner or a principal of the ownership entity directly oversees operations — handling leasing, maintenance calls, vendor relationships, and compliance obligations personally or with minimal staff. This works for some owners with small portfolios and hands-on involvement, but it scales poorly and creates concentration risk. It also places the full burden of DC, Virginia, or Maryland regulatory compliance directly on the owner without professional support.

**In-house management** means the owner employs their own property management staff — a property manager, leasing agents, maintenance technicians — directly on their payroll. This provides control and integration with the ownership entity but requires the owner to function as an employer, managing HR, benefits, training, supervision, and turnover. In-house management can make sense for large enough portfolios to support dedicated staff, but it comes with fixed overhead that doesn't flex with occupancy or revenue.

**Third-party management** transfers operational responsibility to a specialized firm. The management company brings existing systems, trained staff, vendor relationships, and compliance infrastructure. The owner pays a management fee rather than direct employment costs. The model scales — the same management company can serve a 20-unit building and a 200-unit building, applying appropriate staffing to each.

For most multifamily owners in the DC metro market with one to several properties, third-party management offers meaningful operational and compliance advantages that are difficult to replicate at equivalent cost through in-house staffing.

## Why DC Metro Owners Specifically Turn to Third-Party Management

The DC metro market has characteristics that make the case for third-party management particularly strong.

**Regulatory complexity.** Washington DC is one of the most heavily regulated residential rental markets in the country. Multifamily buildings in DC must maintain a valid Basic Business License (BBL) under DC Code § 47-2851.03. Buildings constructed before 1975 that aren't exempt are likely subject to rent stabilization under DC Code § 42-3501 et seq., affecting rent pricing, annual increase limits, and mandatory annual registrations. The Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act (TOPA, DC Code § 42-3404) creates formal obligations when ownership changes. Fair housing, lead paint, habitability, tenant screening, and notice requirements all carry compliance obligations specific to DC law.

Managing a DC multifamily building without current, working knowledge of these requirements creates real financial and legal exposure. A qualified third-party management company brings institutional compliance knowledge and applies it consistently across the portfolio — it's part of what you're paying for.

**Northern Virginia compliance considerations.** Virginia residential properties are governed by the Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA), which applies to properties with more than four units. Security deposit rules, maintenance obligations, lease requirements, and eviction procedures all have VRLTA-specific standards that differ from DC. Arlington, Fairfax, Alexandria, and the Tysons corridor all have active rental markets where operational compliance matters and where competition for residents is real.

**Maryland market specifics.** Maryland multifamily properties in the Bethesda, Potomac, and broader Montgomery County and Prince George's County markets operate under Maryland state law, with additional county-level licensing and regulatory overlays. Montgomery County in particular has active rent stabilization and tenant protection policies that owners should be managing proactively.

**Leasing competition.** The DC metro rental market is competitive, and leasing velocity matters for NOI. Professional leasing infrastructure — pricing analysis, multi-platform marketing, responsive showings, coordinated application processing — makes a material difference in minimizing vacancy and attracting qualified residents. Third-party management companies with active, established leasing operations typically outperform individually managed buildings in time-to-lease and application quality.

## What Third-Party Management Services Should Include

Not all third-party management agreements are structured the same way. Owners should understand what's included in the management fee versus what's billed separately. A well-structured management relationship for DC metro multifamily should include:

**Leasing and marketing.** Listing coordination, pricing analysis, showings, application processing, tenant screening (including compliance with DC's Fair Criminal Record Screening Act and VA/MD equivalent requirements), lease execution, and move-in coordination.

**Rent collection and financial management.** Monthly rent collection, delinquency management, resident communication regarding payment, monthly financial statements to ownership, annual reconciliations, and support for tax reporting.

**Maintenance coordination.** Work order tracking, vendor dispatch, emergency maintenance response, preventive maintenance scheduling, and capital project coordination. Owners should specifically ask whether the management company marks up vendor invoices (many do) and whether original vendor invoices are provided.

**Regulatory compliance monitoring.** License renewals (BBL and any applicable local licenses), rent stabilization registration and filing in DC, lease compliance with jurisdiction-specific notice requirements, lead paint compliance, fair housing training and practices.

**Owner reporting.** Regular financial reporting (monthly at minimum), occupancy and leasing reports, maintenance summary reporting, and budget variance analysis.

**Resident communication.** Handling day-to-day resident inquiries, maintenance requests, lease renewals, and notice delivery.

## What the Owner Retains

Third-party management transfers day-to-day operational responsibility — it doesn't transfer ownership authority or decision-making on material matters. In a well-structured management relationship, the owner retains:

- Final approval on annual operating budgets
- Decision authority on capital expenditures above a defined threshold
- Control over major vendor selection and contract approvals above a defined value
- Strategic decisions about disposition, refinancing, or repositioning
- Ultimate responsibility for regulatory compliance (the management company executes; the owner is the responsible party under law)

A management agreement that blurs these lines — giving the management company too much unilateral authority — puts the owner in a passive position that isn't appropriate. A management agreement that's too restrictive — requiring owner approval for routine maintenance decisions — undermines the operational benefit of professional management. Define the authority thresholds clearly in the agreement.

## Fee Structure — What Third-Party Management Actually Costs

Third-party property management fees for multifamily assets in the DC metro area are typically structured around:

**Management fee.** Usually expressed as a percentage of collected revenue, typically in the range of 6% to 10% for smaller multifamily properties (under 50 units), with some compression for larger portfolios. Some management companies charge flat monthly fees per unit rather than a percentage. The critical distinction: fees should be based on collected rent, not scheduled rent — you should not be paying a management fee on uncollected rent or during vacancy.

**Leasing fees.** Typically charged when a new tenant is placed — commonly one half to one month's rent per unit placed. Lease renewal fees are also common and are typically lower than new leasing fees.

**Maintenance markups.** Some management companies markup vendor invoices by 10–20% (or more). This is a significant, often underappreciated cost for owners with older buildings or high maintenance volume. Ask directly whether markups apply and whether original vendor invoices will be provided.

**Miscellaneous fees.** Vacancy fees, administrative fees, setup fees, termination fees — review the full agreement for fees outside the headline management rate.

A transparent fee structure with full invoice visibility is worth paying for. Owners who choose management companies based purely on the lowest headline management percentage — without understanding the full fee structure — often find that the true cost of management is higher than they expected.

## Key Performance Metrics to Track

Once you've engaged third-party management, the quality of the relationship depends on whether you're measuring the right things. For DC metro multifamily, key metrics include:

**Occupancy and vacancy rate.** Target occupancy of 93–95% or better for most stabilized multifamily assets. Sustained vacancy above 5–7% warrants conversation with the management company about pricing strategy, marketing effectiveness, and unit condition.

**Days to lease.** Average time from a unit becoming available to an executed lease. DC metro market conditions vary by submarket; your management company should be able to benchmark your building's leasing velocity against comparable properties.

**Resident retention / renewal rate.** Renewals are significantly less expensive than turnovers. A management company that invests in resident satisfaction and timely, proactive renewal outreach will deliver better NOI outcomes than one that treats renewal as an afterthought.

**Maintenance response times.** Emergency maintenance response should be measurable in hours, not days. Routine maintenance should be resolved within a defined window. Track actual versus commitment.

**Delinquency rate.** Percentage of total rent that is outstanding at end of month. Consistent delinquency above 2–3% warrants scrutiny of collection practices.

**Owner financial reporting.** Monthly financial statements should be delivered on schedule, in a format you can actually read and act on. If you're consistently chasing reports or can't reconcile your financials, the management relationship isn't working.

## How to Evaluate a Third-Party Management Company for Your Building

Before hiring a third-party management company for a DC metro multifamily asset, ask:

1. **Do they hold the required licenses?** DC property managers must hold a DC real estate broker's license. Verify it through DLCP before engaging.

2. **What is their current DC multifamily portfolio, and what is average occupancy across those properties?** Ask for specifics, not testimonials.

3. **How do they handle DC rent stabilization compliance?** This is a binary question for pre-1975 DC buildings — either they have an operational process for it or they don't.

4. **What does their maintenance infrastructure look like?** In-house staff or outside vendors? What's their emergency response protocol? Do they mark up vendor invoices?

5. **What's included in the management fee versus billed separately?** Get a complete fee schedule in writing before signing.

6. **What does the management agreement termination clause look like?** How much notice is required? Are there termination fees? Can you exit if performance standards aren't met?

7. **Who is your specific point of contact, and how is your account staffed?** A management company that assigns your building to an overloaded portfolio manager will underperform regardless of their company-level reputation.

## Frequently Asked Questions for Multifamily Owners

**Is third-party management required for DC multifamily properties?**  
No. DC law does not require that you hire a third-party management company. However, the operator of a DC rental property must have a valid DC Business License, and individuals who collect rent or manage property on behalf of others must hold a DC real estate broker's license. If you're self-managing and don't personally hold a broker license, you can manage your own property — but you cannot delegate that management to an unlicensed party.

**What size multifamily property makes third-party management worthwhile?**  
There's no universal threshold, but most owners find that the economics of third-party management begin to make clear sense at four to six units or more, where the operational time commitment becomes significant and the cost of compliance errors begins to exceed the management fee. For DC properties specifically — where rent stabilization, BBL requirements, and TOPA create real compliance obligations — the break-even point for professional management tends to come earlier than in less regulated markets.

**Can third-party management help us with a DC rent-stabilized building?**  
Yes. A management company with DC multifamily experience should handle rent stabilization registration, allowable increase calculations, annual filings with the DC Rental Accommodations Division, and any related compliance documentation as part of standard management services. This is one of the clearest areas where professional management creates tangible value for DC building owners.

**What happens to the management relationship if we decide to sell?**  
The management agreement should address this directly. Most agreements include a transition process and a notice period (typically 30–60 days) when an owner decides to sell or terminate the relationship. DC's TOPA obligations are triggered by the sale decision, and your management company should be able to help you navigate the tenant notification process. Review the management agreement's termination provisions before signing, not after you've decided to sell.

**How do I compare management companies beyond price?**  
Beyond the fee structure, the most meaningful differentiators are: operational depth in your specific jurisdiction, maintenance infrastructure and vendor relationships, leasing performance data from comparable properties, financial reporting quality, and the quality and experience of the specific person who will manage your account. Ask to speak with owners of comparable properties in their portfolio — not just references the company selects for you.
