# Washington, DC HOA and Condo Laws: An Operational Guide for Board Members

Serving on the board of a condominium or homeowners association in Washington, DC, is a significant responsibility. Board members are tasked with protecting property values, managing large budgets, and ensuring the community operates smoothly. However, doing so requires strict adherence to local regulations.

In the District of Columbia, community associations are primarily governed by the **DC Condominium Act (D.C. Code § 42-1901 et seq.)** and the **DC Nonprofit Corporation Act**. Understanding these laws is not just for attorneys—it is essential for everyday board operations.

## The DC Condominium Act: Core Operational Impacts

The DC Condominium Act is the foundational legal framework for condos in the District. It dictates everything from how meetings must be run to how assessments are collected.

### 1. Open Meetings and Transparency
Under the Act, all meetings of the board of directors must be open to unit owners. While boards can enter into "executive session" (closed-door meetings) for specific, sensitive reasons—such as discussing pending litigation, personnel matters, or individual owner delinquencies—the general business of the association must be conducted in public.
*   **Operational Tip:** Your management company should help you draft clear agendas and ensure proper notice is given for all meetings (typically 14 to 30 days for annual meetings) to prevent owners from challenging board decisions based on procedural errors.

### 2. Reserve Funds and Capital Planning
One of the most critical fiduciary duties of a board is maintaining adequate reserve funds for the repair and replacement of common elements (e.g., roofs, elevators, HVAC systems). While the DC Condominium Act does not explicitly mandate a specific funding percentage, it requires boards to act with the care of a "reasonably prudent person." Failing to fund reserves adequately can be viewed as a breach of this fiduciary duty.
*   **Operational Tip:** Whether you are a midsize community or a large-scale high-rise, you should commission a professional Reserve Study every 3 to 5 years. Our [HOA Management](/services/hoa-management/washington-dc) teams use these studies to help boards build sustainable, long-term budgets that avoid sudden, massive special assessments.

### 3. Assessment Collection and Liens
When owners fail to pay their condo fees, the entire community suffers. The DC Condominium Act provides associations with a powerful tool: the statutory lien. The association automatically holds a lien against a unit for unpaid assessments. Furthermore, DC law grants a "super-priority" lien for up to six months of unpaid assessments, meaning the association can collect this amount even ahead of the first mortgage holder in the event of a foreclosure.
*   **Operational Tip:** Boards must establish a clear, written collections policy. Your property manager should execute this policy uniformly, sending late notices and coordinating with legal counsel to file liens before the statute of limitations expires (three years in DC).

### 4. Resale Certificates
When a unit is sold, the seller must provide the buyer with a resale certificate (often called a "condo questionnaire" or disclosure packet). This document details the financial health of the association, pending litigation, and any rule violations associated with the specific unit. By law, the association must provide this certificate within 10 days of a request.
*   **Operational Tip:** Delays in providing resale certificates can jeopardize real estate transactions and expose the board to liability. Professional management ensures these documents are generated accurately and promptly.

## Fiduciary Duty: The "Business Judgment Rule"

Board members are volunteers, but they are held to a legal standard known as the "Business Judgment Rule." This means that as long as a board member acts in good faith, in the best interest of the association, and with the care an ordinarily prudent person would exercise, they are generally protected from personal liability for their decisions.

However, this protection evaporates if a board member engages in self-dealing, fraud, or willful negligence. 
*   **When to involve counsel:** If a board decision involves a conflict of interest, a major structural defect, or a dispute that could lead to litigation, you should immediately consult the association's legal counsel.
