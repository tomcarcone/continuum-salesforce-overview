# What is an HOA? A Guide for DC, Virginia, and Maryland Associations

A Homeowners Association (HOA) or Condominium Association is a private, governing organization that manages and maintains a residential community. When you purchase a property within an HOA or condo building, you automatically become a member of the association, agree to abide by its rules, and commit to paying mandatory assessments (dues).

While the basic concept of an HOA is universal, how they actually operate—and the laws that govern them—varies significantly depending on whether your community is located in Washington, DC, Virginia, or Maryland. 

For board members tasked with running these communities, understanding the definition of an HOA is just the first step. You must also understand the specific legal and operational frameworks that apply to your jurisdiction.

## The Purpose of an HOA or Condo Association

At its core, an association exists to serve three primary functions:

1.  **Maintain Common Elements:** The association is responsible for the upkeep of shared spaces. In a suburban Virginia HOA, this might mean maintaining community pools, private roads, and landscaping. In a Washington, DC high-rise condo, this means maintaining elevators, the roof, the lobby, and the central HVAC systems.
2.  **Protect Property Values:** By enforcing community rules (covenants, conditions, and restrictions, or CC&Rs) and ensuring the neighborhood or building remains aesthetically pleasing and structurally sound, the HOA protects the investment of all owners.
3.  **Manage Community Finances:** The association collects dues to fund daily operations and builds a reserve fund to pay for long-term capital replacements (like a new roof or repaving a parking lot).

## HOA vs. Condo Association: What’s the Difference?

Though often used interchangeably, there is a distinct legal difference between an HOA and a Condo Association:

*   **Condominium Association:** In a condo, you own the interior of your specific unit (the "walls-in"), but you own a shared percentage of the common elements (the land, the exterior walls, the hallways) jointly with all other owners. 
*   **Homeowners Association (HOA):** In a traditional HOA (like a neighborhood of single-family homes or townhomes), you own your individual lot and the home on it entirely. The HOA itself owns the common areas (like the clubhouse or parks).

## The Legal Frameworks in the DMV

If you serve on an association board, you cannot rely on generic, national advice. Your community is governed by specific state or district laws:

*   **Washington, DC:** Most associations here are condos governed by the **DC Condominium Act**. This act dictates strict rules regarding open board meetings, the collection of assessments, and the timeline for providing resale certificates.
*   **Virginia:** Virginia has distinct laws depending on the property type. Condos are governed by the **Virginia Condominium Act**, while traditional HOAs are governed by the **Property Owners' Association Act (POAA)**. Virginia also requires management companies to hold specific licenses through the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR).
*   **Maryland:** Similar to Virginia, Maryland separates its laws into the **Maryland Condominium Act** and the **Maryland Homeowners Association Act**. Maryland is particularly strict regarding reserve study requirements and the mandatory funding of those reserves.

## The Role of the Board of Directors

An HOA is a corporate entity (usually a non-profit), and it requires a Board of Directors to function. Board members are volunteers elected by the community. They possess a fiduciary duty to act in the best financial and operational interests of the association.

Their duties include:
*   Drafting and approving the annual budget
*   Setting the monthly HOA fees
*   Hiring vendors (landscapers, janitorial staff, contractors)
*   Enforcing the community's rules and architectural guidelines
*   Hiring and overseeing a professional property management company
